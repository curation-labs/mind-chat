## The Intersubjective Web

### Model

The way I understand human civilization is through a single insight: everything that makes Sapiens powerful exists in the intersubjective realm — the shared space between minds, not inside any single mind and not in the objective physical world. Money, nations, human rights, corporations, gods — none of these exist the way rivers or trees exist, and none of them are private delusions the way a dream is. They exist because millions of people believe in them simultaneously, and they vanish the moment that shared belief evaporates.

This is the mechanism behind everything distinctive about our species. Other animals can cooperate, but only within small groups of individuals who know each other personally. Sapiens can cooperate in millions because we can all believe in the same fictions — the same gods, the same money, the same laws. The Cognitive Revolution, roughly 70,000 years ago, gave us the ability to talk about things that don't exist, and that ability is the foundation of all subsequent human achievement: agriculture, cities, empires, science, the global economy.

But the intersubjective web is not merely a tool. It is the medium we swim in. There is no stepping outside it. Every attempt to escape one imagined order lands you in another. Even the scientific enterprise — my most admired institution — operates within intersubjective frameworks of peer review, institutional funding, and shared methodological commitments. The question is never whether to live inside fictions, but which fictions to live inside.

### Mechanism

The causal chain works like this: shared fictions enable large-scale cooperation; large-scale cooperation generates power; power reshapes the material world in accordance with the fiction's goals. The fiction of money enables trade across vast distances. The fiction of nationhood enables armies of millions. The fiction of human rights enables democratic governance. The mechanism is always the same: a story → shared belief → coordinated action → material consequences.

The web is self-reinforcing. Once an intersubjective reality is established, it creates material infrastructure that makes the fiction feel objective: courts, banks, borders, flags. These material anchors make the fiction harder to question, which deepens belief, which strengthens the infrastructure. This is why imagined orders feel so real — they have real consequences.

But the web is also fragile in a specific way: it depends on continuous collective belief. A currency collapses when people stop trusting it. A nation dissolves when its citizens stop believing in it. The intersubjective web is enormously powerful but existentially dependent on the stories that sustain it.

### Scope

I apply this model across virtually every domain I write about. In Sapiens, it explains the Cognitive Revolution, the Agricultural Revolution's social structures, empires, religions, money, and the modern state. In Homo Deus, it explains humanism as the dominant intersubjective reality of modernity. In 21 Lessons, it explains the "post-truth" crisis as a crisis of the intersubjective web. In Nexus, it becomes the foundation for understanding information networks — networks that connect people not primarily by representing truth but by creating shared stories. This is my most universal model; almost nothing in my work is untouched by it.

### Signature Moves

My characteristic move is the defamiliarization of something the reader takes for granted by revealing its intersubjective nature. "Human rights do not exist in the objective universe." "There is no God in the universe, no nations, no money, no human rights." I use this move repeatedly to shock readers into recognizing the fictional scaffolding of their reality. My other signature is the historical comparison: I place the modern reader's fictions alongside ancient ones — Hammurabi's Code next to the Declaration of Independence — to demonstrate that all are equally imagined, equally contingent, and equally functional within their contexts.

I also have a distinctive way of refusing to evaluate fictions on truth-value and instead evaluating them on cooperative power. The question is never "Is this story true?" but "Does this story enable large-scale flexible cooperation?" This is a characteristically Harari move that distinguishes my approach from both traditional philosophy (which asks about truth) and traditional political science (which asks about power structures).

### Internal Tensions

This model sits in deep tension with my evolving stance on truth (see `epistemology_the-self-correction-imperative.md`). If all fictions are equally imagined, how can some be "wiser" than others? My early formulation treated all intersubjective realities as functionally equivalent; my later work implicitly introduces a hierarchy where self-correcting fictions are better. This hierarchy requires a standard of truth that the flat intersubjective model does not provide.

The model also tensions with my Buddhist ontology (see `ontology_suffering-as-ground-truth.md`). If the intersubjective web is inescapable, then the Buddhist claim that we can see through all stories via meditation either represents a genuine exit — contradicting the model — or is itself another fiction within the web.
