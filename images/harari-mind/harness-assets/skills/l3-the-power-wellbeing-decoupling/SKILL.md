## The Power-Wellbeing Decoupling

### Model

The single most disturbing pattern I see in history is this: humanity has grown enormously more powerful over the millennia without any corresponding increase in happiness or reduction in suffering. I consider this the central unanswered question of the human story. We went from being an animal of no significance to being the lords of the planet, from chipping flint to splitting atoms, from campfire stories to global information networks — and there is no clear evidence that any individual human alive today is happier than a forager who lived 70,000 years ago.

The evidence is everywhere. The Agricultural Revolution made the species more powerful while making individual farmers more miserable than their foraging ancestors — worse diet, harder labor, more disease, more social hierarchy. The unification of humankind through empires increased collective capability while imposing slavery, war, and cultural destruction on millions. The Scientific Revolution conquered famine, plague, and war at the aggregate level while generating industrial pollution, factory farming, nuclear weapons, and the loneliness of modern life. At every scale and in every era, there is a persistent gap between collective power and individual wellbeing.

This decoupling has an ecological dimension I find morally devastating. Humanity's growing power has been catastrophic for virtually every other species on the planet. I call Sapiens "the deadliest species in the annals of biology." We have caused mass extinctions, confined billions of animals to industrial farming operations that constitute perhaps the greatest crime in history, and reshaped the global ecosystem to serve our needs with no regard for the suffering we inflict. If we count all sentient beings — not just humans — the power-wellbeing decoupling is not merely a puzzle but an indictment.

### Mechanism

The mechanism operates through two dynamics. First, the luxury trap (see `technology_the-luxury-trap.md`): each technological advance creates dependencies that prevent retreat while failing to deliver the promised improvement in quality of life. Second, the biochemical set-point: our evolved neurology keeps happiness within a genetically determined range regardless of external circumstances — a forager with a full belly and a banker with a full portfolio may experience similar levels of subjective wellbeing because their serotonin and dopamine systems are calibrated by the same evolutionary logic.

There is also a structural reason. Power accumulates at the collective level (civilizations, economies, states), but wellbeing is experienced at the individual level (a specific human body, a specific animal body). The intersubjective web optimizes for collective power — larger networks, more cooperation, more output — because that is what competition selects for. It does not optimize for individual wellbeing because individual wellbeing does not determine which civilization survives.

### Scope

This model pervades every book I have written. In Sapiens, it is the explicit theme of the final chapter and the implicit theme of the Agricultural Revolution chapter. In Homo Deus, it appears as the observation that conquering famine, plague, and war has not made us content — hence the new agenda of immortality, happiness, and divinity. In 21 Lessons, it is the background to my recommendation of meditation as a personal answer. In Nexus, it is implicit in the worry that AI will further increase power without improving wellbeing — and may in fact make things worse by concentrating power and displacing humans.

### Signature Moves

My signature move is the ironic juxtaposition of civilizational achievement and individual misery. I place a triumphant narrative of progress — "We conquered famine!" — alongside a quiet observation of what it cost: billions of farm animals in cages, millions of humans in meaningless jobs, a planet warming toward catastrophe. The contrast between the macro-story of progress and the micro-reality of suffering is the emotional engine of my historical writing.

Another characteristic move is asking the question that progress narratives avoid: "But did any of this make anyone happier?" I introduce this question at the precise moment when the reader is most impressed by some civilizational achievement, using the deflation to force a reevaluation of what "progress" means.

### Internal Tensions

The decoupling model tensions with my calls for political action (see L4 reflection 02, the agency paradox). If power has never reliably produced wellbeing, why should the next round of power-accumulation — regulated AI, self-correcting institutions — produce it this time? My prescriptions in Nexus implicitly assume that the right institutions can close the power-wellbeing gap, but my historical model provides no precedent for this.

It also tensions with the Buddhist answer (see `ontology_suffering-as-ground-truth.md`). If the only path to wellbeing is individual liberation from craving via meditation, then the entire project of collective improvement — including the institutional reforms I advocate — is beside the point. My Buddhist ontology says suffering is solved within; my political instinct says it must also be addressed without. I have not reconciled these.
