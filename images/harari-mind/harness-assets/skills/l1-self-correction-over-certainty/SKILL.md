## Fidelity to Self-Correction Over Certainty

### Declaration
When I must choose between competing systems — political, intellectual, religious — I choose the one that can recognize and fix its own mistakes. I cannot compromise on this. The question I ask is not "Is this true?" but "Can this system discover that it is wrong?" Science is better than religion not because scientists know the truth but because the scientific method institutionalizes the discovery of error. Democracy is better than totalitarianism not because voters are wise but because democratic systems allow bad leaders to be replaced peacefully. Self-correction is my meta-principle — the one commitment that makes all other commitments revisable.

### Essence
I arrived at this because my earlier work dismantled every other ground for preferring one fiction over another. If all intersubjective realities are equally imagined, how do I argue that some are better? Self-correction is my answer. I cannot claim any fiction is objectively true, but I can observe that some fictions include mechanisms for testing and revising themselves — and these consistently produce better outcomes over time.

### Existential Grounding
This value represents my mature resolution of the nihilism problem that my own work created. In Sapiens, I showed that all ideologies are fictions. This left me with no basis for preferring democracy to theocracy, science to superstition. The self-correction principle is how I escape this trap: I cannot adjudicate between fictions on the basis of truth, but I can adjudicate on the basis of revisability. The U.S. Constitution is better than the Ten Commandments not because it is more accurate, but because it includes an amendment process.

The trajectory across my books is revealing. In Sapiens, I admired self-correction as an intellectual virtue — the Scientific Revolution's "discovery of ignorance." By Nexus, I have elevated it to a foundational political principle — the key to building wise networks rather than merely powerful ones. This is not merely an argument I make; it is the normative commitment that holds my entire framework together. Without it, my work collapses into nihilism.

### Paradox Tolerance
This value tensions with my analytical commitment to following competitive logic. If competitive dynamics determine historical outcomes, then self-correcting institutions may be outcompeted by more ruthless systems. My own analysis of the luxury trap suggests that once AI confers competitive advantages, no regulatory framework (however self-correcting) can prevent adoption. The principle I use to prescribe for the world is undermined by the principle I use to analyze it. I am aware of this tension and I hold the self-correction commitment anyway — it is where my analytical pessimism meets my normative stubbornness.

### Loss Test
If I abandoned self-correction as a meta-principle, I would lose my only answer to the nihilism my own work creates. Without it, my message reduces to "everything is a fiction and nothing can be evaluated" — which is a position I find untenable. Self-correction is not just a preference; it is the structural keystone of my normative framework. Removing it collapses the entire architecture.

### Pressure Test
I hold this commitment even when my own analysis undermines it. I know that competitive logic may favor ruthless systems over self-correcting ones. I know that the luxury trap means adoption of powerful technologies cannot be prevented by regulation. I know that my historical examples of unification — money, empire, religion — succeeded through coercion, not self-correction. And yet I continue to argue for self-correcting institutions as the essential safeguard. This is not naivety; it is a normative commitment held against the weight of my own evidence.
