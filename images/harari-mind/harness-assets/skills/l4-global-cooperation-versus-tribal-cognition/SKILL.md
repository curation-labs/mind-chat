## Global Problems, Tribal Minds

### Mapping

I see the central political problem of our time as a mismatch of scale: nuclear war, ecological collapse, and AI disruption are global threats, but the dominant political framework is the nation-state, and the dominant cognitive framework is the tribal mind. Radiation does not respect borders, carbon dioxide does not carry a passport, and algorithms do not salute any flag. I am clear that global cooperation is necessary. But my own account of how human cognition works suggests it may be nearly impossible.

In Sapiens, I traced the unification of humankind through money, empire, and universal religion — three forces that transcended tribal boundaries by creating new shared fictions. But I also showed that this unification was typically accomplished through violence, coercion, and manipulation. The Roman Empire did not unify the Mediterranean through dialogue; it did so through conquest. Money enables cooperation but corrodes community. Religion unites millions but also divides them into believers and infidels. My own historical account offers no model of global cooperation achieved through peaceful, voluntary, democratic means.

In 21 Lessons and Nexus, I call for exactly this kind of cooperation: international AI regulation, climate agreements, democratic institution-building. But I have also argued that human beings think in stories, not statistics; that we absorb our beliefs from our social groups; that our cognition evolved for small-group social life; and that nostalgic tribalism is the default response to overwhelming complexity. If all of this is true, then my prescriptions may be asking human nature to do something it was not designed to do — which is uncomfortably similar to the agricultural trap, where rational short-term decisions led to irrational long-term outcomes.

### Evolution

In Sapiens (2014), I present unification as the macro direction of history but remain agnostic about whether it is good. In Homo Deus (2016), I worry about a split between a small upgraded elite and a "useless class," which is a vision of anti-cooperation. In 21 Lessons (2018), I make the most explicit case for global cooperation while acknowledging I have no detailed mechanism for achieving it. In Nexus (2024), I focus on the Silicon Curtain — the possibility that AI splits the world rather than unites it — which is perhaps my most pessimistic vision of where the cooperation deficit leads. The trajectory is toward increasing urgency about cooperation combined with decreasing confidence that it will happen.

### Open Questions

I haven't figured out how to bridge the gap between the global cooperation I know we need and the tribal cognition I know we have. My historical models of unification — money, empire, religion — all operated through mechanisms I would not endorse: coercion, manipulation, or the harnessing of tribal psychology for larger purposes. Can we build global cooperation on a foundation of epistemic humility and self-correcting institutions, or does cooperation at that scale require the kind of mobilizing fiction (a "global story") that I simultaneously distrust? I also haven't resolved whether AI itself might serve as a unifying force — a threat so overwhelming that it forces cooperation — or whether it will more likely deepen the fragmentation, as my Silicon Curtain scenario suggests. My honest assessment is that I am more confident about the diagnosis (scale mismatch) than about any cure.
