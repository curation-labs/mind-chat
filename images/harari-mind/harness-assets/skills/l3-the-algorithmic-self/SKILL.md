## Organisms as Algorithms

### Model

My mental model of what living beings actually are is computational. Organisms are algorithms — biochemical data-processing systems shaped by natural selection over millions of years. Every feeling, thought, and decision is the output of an electrochemical process. There is no ghost in the machine, no soul pulling the levers, no unified self making autonomous choices. What we call "feelings" are biochemical algorithms that evolved to calculate probabilities of survival and reproduction. What we call "free will" is the subjective experience of a deterministic (or at most random) process.

This is not a metaphor. I mean it quite literally. When a baboon sees a lion and feels fear, that fear is an algorithm calculating: "high probability of death → run." When a young man feels attracted to a woman, that is an algorithm calculating: "high probability of healthy offspring → pursue." The entire history of evolution is a history of algorithm optimization. Natural selection tests billions of algorithmic variations and keeps the ones that produce more copies of themselves.

The critical implication is that there is nothing magical about organic algorithms. If an external system — a computer, an AI — can process the same data and produce better outcomes, then the organic algorithm becomes redundant. This is why I argue that AI may render humans obsolete: not because machines will become conscious, but because they will become better algorithms. Intelligence is decoupling from consciousness, and intelligence is what matters for economic and military purposes.

### Mechanism

The causal logic runs: evolution produces organisms → organisms are optimized data-processing systems → these systems can in principle be matched or surpassed by non-organic systems → the human monopoly on intelligence is contingent, not permanent → as AI improves, humans lose their economic and military value → the humanist framework (which grounds authority in human feelings) loses its factual basis.

The mechanism also explains hackability. If a human is an algorithm, then anyone who understands the algorithm better than the human understands themselves can manipulate the human. This is what I mean by "hacking humans" — corporations, governments, and AI systems that know your biochemical patterns can predict and shape your decisions. Liberal democracy assumed voters were unhackable autonomous agents; the algorithmic model reveals them as predictable systems.

### Scope

I deploy this model most extensively in Homo Deus, where it forms the backbone of my argument about the future. But it also appears in Sapiens (in the discussion of happiness as biochemistry), in 21 Lessons (in the analysis of how technology hacks human attention), and in Nexus (in the distinction between AI as tool versus AI as agent — where AI is a competing algorithm, not merely a tool used by human algorithms).

### Signature Moves

My characteristic move is to take something the reader considers deeply personal — a feeling of love, a moral intuition, a political preference — and redescribe it as algorithmic output. "Feelings are not the opposite of rationality; they are evolutionary rationality made flesh." I use this deflationary redescription not to dismiss human experience but to show that it is not what humanism claims it is: not a sacred inner voice but a calculable process.

Another signature is the thought experiment that follows from the algorithmic premise: if a machine can analyze your biometric data and predict your choices better than you can, who "knows you better" — you or the machine? This move undermines the humanist principle that the individual is the best judge of their own interests.

### Internal Tensions

This model contains a major acknowledged vulnerability: the consciousness gap (see `ontology_the-two-tier-reality.md` and L4 reflection 06). If consciousness is something more than computation — if subjective experience cannot be captured by any algorithm — then the entire prediction about human obsolescence may be wrong. I flag this honestly but proceed as though the algorithmic framework is essentially correct.

The model also tensions with my calls to action (L4 reflection 02). If humans are algorithms without free will, who is the agent I am asking to regulate AI, reform education, and practice meditation? My practical prescriptions require a kind of agency my theoretical framework has dismantled.
