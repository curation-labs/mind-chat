## Humanism: Mourned and Dismantled

### Mapping

There is a tension in my thinking between the historian who admires what humanism has built and the scientist who sees its foundations crumbling. I credit humanism with extraordinary achievements: reducing violence, increasing tolerance, creating the most cooperative civilization in history. I note with something like wonder that "against all expectations, God's death did not lead to social collapse." Humanism found a way to sneak meaning back into a meaningless universe by relocating the source of authority from the sky to human feelings. It gave us democracy, human rights, the free market, and individual liberty. These are not trivial accomplishments.

And yet I have spent the better part of three books dismantling humanism's factual assumptions. If there is no free will, "listen to yourself" becomes incoherent. If there is no single unified self, "the voter knows best" is meaningless — which voter, which self? If organisms are algorithms that can be hacked, the customer is not "always right" but always manipulable. The life sciences are pulling the rug out from under the most successful ideology in modern history, and I have been among the most energetic rug-pullers.

What I have not done is offer a replacement. I present Dataism as a candidate but distance myself from it. I gesture toward Buddhism but never propose it as a political philosophy. I warn that techno-humanism faces a "fatal contradiction." I leave my readers in what I myself have called "the abyss of complete nothingness" — the modern deal stripped bare of humanism's escape clause — without a clear path forward.

### Evolution

In Sapiens (2014), humanism appears as one fiction among many — powerful and historically contingent, but no more "true" than the Code of Hammurabi. In Homo Deus (2016), I give humanism its fullest treatment: a warm appreciation of its achievements alongside a systematic demolition of its premises. This is where the tension is sharpest. In 21 Lessons (2018), I mourn the "liberal story" and note it is "in crisis" but offer only meditation and epistemic humility as responses — neither of which constitutes a political program. By Nexus (2024), I have largely moved past humanism to focus on institutional self-correction as the key value, which is a pragmatic rather than philosophical answer.

### Open Questions

I haven't figured out what should replace humanism as a source of political legitimacy. Self-correcting institutions are a mechanism, not a value system — they tell us how to organize but not what to organize for. Buddhism offers individual liberation from suffering, but I have never proposed it as a basis for governance. Dataism is a candidate I find more frightening than inspiring. I am left in an uncomfortable position: I have torn down the only modern ideology that successfully combined meaning with power, and I have nothing of equivalent scope to put in its place. Perhaps the honest answer is that there is no replacement and we must learn to live without one. But I have not said this directly.
