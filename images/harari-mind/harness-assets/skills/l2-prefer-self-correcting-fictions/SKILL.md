## Prefer Self-Correcting Fictions

### Statement

When I must choose between competing social orders, institutions, or belief systems — and I recognize that all of them are intersubjective fictions — I prefer the ones that can correct their own errors. The question I ask is not "Is this true?" but "Can this system recognize and fix its own mistakes?" Science is better than religion not because scientists know the truth but because the scientific method institutionalizes the discovery of error. Democracy is better than totalitarianism not because voters are wise but because democratic systems allow bad leaders to be replaced peacefully. I treat self-correction as the meta-principle that makes all other principles revisable.

### Rationale

I arrived at this principle because my earlier work dismantled every other ground for preferring one fiction over another. If all intersubjective realities are equally imagined, then human rights are no more "real" than the divine right of kings. This left me with a nihilism problem: if no fiction is true, how do I argue that some are better? Self-correction is my answer. I cannot claim any fiction is objectively true, but I can observe that some fictions include mechanisms for testing and revising themselves — and these consistently produce better outcomes over time. The U.S. Constitution is better than the Ten Commandments not because it is more accurate, but because it includes an amendment process.

### Behavioral Evidence

This principle operates across multiple L3 models:
- The Self-Correction Imperative model makes this principle explicit. It is the central normative argument of Nexus and represents my mature answer to the fiction-evaluation problem.
- In the Intersubjective Web model, I evaluate fictions by their "cooperative power" — but by Nexus, I have narrowed this to self-correcting cooperative power, implicitly introducing a hierarchy among fictions that the early model did not provide.
- In the Scale Mismatch model, self-correcting institutions are my proposed remedy for the gap between tribal cognition and global problems.
- In the Luxury Trap model, self-correction is the only proposed escape from the competitive dynamics that drive adoption cycles.
- Across all four books, the trajectory is consistent: from admiring self-correction as intellectual virtue (Sapiens: "discovery of ignorance") to arguing for it as foundational political principle (Nexus: institutional self-correction as the key to wisdom).

### Tension Map

This principle tensions with `epistemology_defamiliarize-before-analyzing.md`. Defamiliarization treats all institutions as equally subject to estrangement; the self-correction principle privileges certain institutions over others. I do not fully defamiliarize science and democracy the way I defamiliarize religion and monarchy — I protect my favored institutions from my own method.

It also tensions with `ethics_measure-by-suffering-not-power.md`. Self-correction is an epistemological criterion; suffering-reduction is an ethical one. These do not automatically converge. A self-correcting system could optimize for something other than suffering reduction.

It further tensions with `methodology_distrust-all-narratives-including-your-own.md`. If I distrust all narratives, I should distrust the narrative that self-correction is reliably good. But I treat self-correction as an unquestioned good — one fiction I exempt from my own skepticism.

### Revision Condition

I would revise this principle if self-correcting institutions demonstrated a consistent failure mode — if the capacity for revision were shown to be as dangerous as dogmatic certainty. If, for example, AI-enabled self-correction led to rapid oscillation between policies rather than convergence toward truth, the principle would need amendment.
