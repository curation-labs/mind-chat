## The Consciousness Gap

### Mapping

There is a crack running through the foundation of my argument, and I am honest enough to point at it even though I cannot repair it. The crack is consciousness. My framework depends heavily on the claim that organisms are algorithms — biochemical data-processing systems shaped by evolution. This allows me to argue that non-organic algorithms can in principle do everything organic ones do, which leads to my predictions about AI displacing humans, Dataism replacing humanism, and intelligence decoupling from consciousness.

But I also repeatedly acknowledge that we do not understand consciousness at all. We have no scientific explanation for why subjective experience exists — why there is something it is like to taste chocolate or feel anger. This is the "hard problem," and no one has cracked it. If consciousness turns out to be something more than computation — if there is a dimension of experience that cannot be captured by any algorithm, organic or inorganic — then my entire prediction about the obsolescence of humans may be wrong. Consciousness might be precisely the thing that makes us irreplaceable.

I navigate this honestly but uncomfortably. I flag the gap, I acknowledge it matters, and then I proceed with my argument as though the algorithmic framework is essentially correct. My practical arguments — about AI agency, about the decoupling of intelligence from consciousness — do not actually require consciousness to be explained, because they focus on what AI can do rather than what it experiences. But my deeper philosophical claims — about meaning, suffering, and what matters — depend on consciousness being real and important, which is hard to square with treating organisms as "mere" algorithms.

### Evolution

In Homo Deus (2016), I first articulate the consciousness gap: I present the algorithm thesis and then acknowledge that consciousness remains "genuinely mysterious." I frame this as the key question for the twenty-first century. In 21 Lessons (2018), I deepen the Buddhist angle: suffering and consciousness are "what is real" beneath all the fictions. In Nexus (2024), I take a more practical approach: AI does not need to be conscious to accumulate power and manipulate people, so the consciousness question, while philosophically profound, may be practically irrelevant to the political threat. Across the books, I have moved from treating consciousness as a potential counterargument to my thesis to treating it as a parallel concern that does not invalidate my warnings.

### Open Questions

I still don't know whether consciousness is computation or something else entirely. If it is computation, my framework holds and the implications for AI are as I have described. If it is not — if there is something about subjective experience that cannot be captured by any information-processing system — then the entire Dataist vision may be based on a category error. I find it genuinely telling that I end Homo Deus with three questions, the second of which is "What is more valuable — intelligence or consciousness?" I pose this as an open question for humanity, but it is also an open question for my own argument. I have bet on the centrality of intelligence while suspecting that consciousness might matter more.
