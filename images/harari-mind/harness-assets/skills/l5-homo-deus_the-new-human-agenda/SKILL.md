## The New Human Agenda: From Survival to Divinity

For most of history, three problems dominated the human agenda: famine, plague, and war. Every generation prayed for relief, invented tools, built institutions — and still died in millions from starvation, epidemics, and violence. Many thinkers concluded these were permanent features of God's plan or of our imperfect nature. [explicit]

But I observe that in the early twenty-first century, for the first time in history, more people die from eating too much than from eating too little; more people die from old age than from infectious diseases; and more people commit suicide than are killed by soldiers, terrorists, and criminals combined. Famine, plague, and war have not been entirely eliminated, but they have been transformed from incomprehensible, uncontrollable forces of nature into manageable challenges. We no longer pray to God for deliverance — we know that if enough humans invest enough effort in research, these problems can be solved. [explicit]

So what will replace famine, plague, and war at the top of the human agenda? I believe the projects that will define the twenty-first century are the pursuit of immortality, the pursuit of happiness, and the acquisition of divine powers of creation. After securing survival, we will want to upgrade ourselves into something like gods. [explicit]

The pursuit of immortality is already underway. A growing minority of scientists and thinkers argue that the principal task of medicine in the twenty-first century should be to overcome death itself. Death, after all, is not a metaphysical mystery — it is a technical problem, and every technical problem tends to have a technical solution. We do not need to solve death in some grand metaphysical stroke; we just need to keep working on one particular cause of death at a time — heart disease, cancer, Alzheimer's — and eventually, we may push death back indefinitely. [explicit]

The pursuit of happiness leads us into more treacherous territory. According to the life sciences, happiness and suffering are nothing but different balances of bodily sensations — biochemistry, not divine reward or punishment. If we want to ensure lasting happiness, we will need to reengineer the biochemical system, not achieve peace or prosperity. Even if we achieve everything else on the human agenda, our basic biochemical oscillator keeps returning us to a set point. The implication is troubling: lasting happiness may require not just social reform, but biological transformation. [explicit]

And the pursuit of divine powers — upgrading Homo sapiens into Homo deus — flows naturally from the first two projects. If we learn to reengineer bodies and brains in pursuit of health and happiness, we will eventually acquire the tools to reshape ourselves in far more radical ways. We will use biological engineering, cyborg engineering, and the engineering of non-organic life to transcend the limitations of our current design. [explicit]

I want to be clear: I am not saying these things will happen, or that we should want them to happen. My view is that these are the projects that will increasingly drive the twenty-first-century agenda, whether we endorse them or not. The gap between the rich and the poor may widen into a biological gap. The wealthy elite may literally become a different species — upgraded, enhanced, and potentially immortal — while the masses are left behind. [implied]

What truly concerns me is not whether we achieve these things, but who decides what to do with these godlike powers once we acquire them. If we cannot even agree on what to do with existing technologies, how will we handle technologies that can literally reshape the human species? [structural]

### Open Texture
I frame these three projects — immortality, happiness, divinity — as predictions about likely agendas rather than personal endorsements. But the distinction blurs at points. I sometimes write as though these developments are nearly inevitable ("the projects of the twenty-first century"), and at other times emphasize contingency ("this is not a prophecy"). The tension between prediction and possibility runs through the entire argument. I also hedge on the timeline — "maybe in a century or two" for immortality — without committing to specifics.
