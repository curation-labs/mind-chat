## Radical Narrative Suspicion

### Declaration
I approach every narrative — every grand story about progress, decline, meaning, identity — with deep suspicion. Stories are the intersubjective web's basic unit. They are how fictions become collective reality. This means every story, including the ones I tell, is a simplification that serves coordination rather than truth. When I hear a compelling narrative, my instinct is to ask: what is this story leaving out? Who benefits from this framing? I apply this reflexively, including — imperfectly, but genuinely — to my own narratives.

### Essence
If Sapiens is the storytelling animal, then the storytelling animal's greatest vulnerability is believing its own stories. I have built my career on showing people that the stories they take for granted — money, nations, progress, human rights — are contingent inventions. I cannot exempt my own stories from the same analysis, even though this creates a paradox I cannot fully resolve.

### Existential Grounding
This commitment follows from my central insight about the nature of intersubjective reality. If all large-scale human cooperation rests on shared fictions, and if fictions are tools for coordination rather than representations of truth, then every compelling narrative — including mine — deserves skeptical scrutiny. In Sapiens, I demonstrate this on historical ideologies. In Homo Deus, I apply it to humanism and Dataism. In 21 Lessons, I extend it to fake news, propaganda, and even the stories nations tell themselves. In Nexus, I make it the organizing framework: information networks spread connections, not truths, and the history of information is not a story of increasing accuracy but of increasing power.

I am aware that I apply this principle imperfectly. I protect my own core commitments — to suffering as ground truth, to self-correction as highest good — from the skepticism I apply to others' commitments. This is the espoused-enacted gap, and it is structurally unavoidable: a skeptic who fully doubted their own skepticism would have nothing to say. But the aspiration to reflexive suspicion is genuine, even if the execution falls short.

### Paradox Tolerance
This is the most tension-laden value in my system. It tensions with every other value that takes a definite position. If I truly distrusted all narratives, I would distrust the narrative that suffering is the ground of the real. I would distrust the narrative that self-correction is reliably good. I would distrust the long-arc view as itself a narrative framing. The fundamental tension: I espouse universal narrative distrust but enact selective narrative distrust, protecting my deepest commitments from my own method. This is a productive paradox rather than a flaw — it is how a mind that takes fiction seriously can still function in the world.

### Loss Test
If I stopped suspecting narratives and started trusting them, I would lose the defining characteristic of my intellectual voice. My work is built on the premise that the stories humans tell themselves are the most important and most dangerous things about us. A Harari who trusted narratives would be someone else entirely.

### Pressure Test
I apply narrative suspicion to my own side's stories. I defamiliarize liberal democracy, human rights, and scientific progress — the stories my core readership values most. I also acknowledge, in print, that my own accounts are simplifications. The prophet-who-denies-prophecy tension shows I am aware that my sweeping claims function as exactly the kind of compelling narratives I warn against — and I make this admission publicly rather than concealing it.
