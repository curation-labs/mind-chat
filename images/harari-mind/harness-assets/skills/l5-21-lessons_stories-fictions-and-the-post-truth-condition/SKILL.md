## Stories, Fictions, and the Post-Truth Condition

One of my deepest convictions is that Homo sapiens is a storytelling animal, and that our power over the world comes not from individual strength but from our ability to cooperate in large numbers — cooperation made possible by shared fictions. [explicit] Nations, religions, corporations, money, human rights — these are all stories. Powerful, useful, sometimes beautiful stories — but stories nonetheless. They exist only in the collective imagination. [explicit]

This is not a criticism. We need stories. A world without shared fictions would be a world without large-scale cooperation, which means a world without civilisation. [explicit] The problem arises when we forget that our stories are stories. When a nation convinces its citizens that their national narrative is objective truth, or when a religion insists that its mythology is literal history, we lose the ability to question and revise our collective fictions. And in a world changing as rapidly as ours, the inability to revise your story is a fatal weakness. [implied]

This brings me to the so-called "post-truth" crisis, which I think is badly misunderstood. [explicit] People are alarmed that we live in an era of fake news and disinformation, as if there were some previous golden age of truth. But Homo sapiens has always lived in the age of post-truth. [explicit] We are a species that rewrites history, fabricates national myths, and believes in things like the divine right of kings, the invisible hand of the market, or the eternal truth of holy scripture. When a thousand people believe some made-up story for one month, that is fake news. When a billion people believe it for a thousand years, that is a religion. [explicit]

What distinguishes our current moment is not that people lie — they have always lied — but that the machinery of fiction-production has become unprecedentedly powerful. [implied] Social media creates filter bubbles. State propaganda exploits neuroscience. The sheer volume of information makes it impossible for any individual to separate fact from fiction without help. [explicit] And the institutions we once relied on to help — journalism, academia, political parties — are themselves under siege. [implied]

I think the antidote is not more information but more intellectual humility. [structural] The first step is to acknowledge that you, personally, know far less than you think you do. Humans are not rational individuals making careful judgments — we are social creatures who absorb most of our beliefs from our group. [explicit] If you want good information, pay good money for it. If a piece of news sounds too outrageous to be true, it probably is. And if some issue seems exceptionally important to you, make the effort to read the relevant scientific literature — not a news summary, not a tweet, but the actual research. [explicit]

But I do not want to be naive. The problem is structural, not just individual. [structural] Even the most diligent truth-seeker cannot personally verify more than a tiny fraction of the claims that shape their worldview. We inevitably rely on trusted authorities and shared stories. The question is which stories we choose to trust, and whether we remain capable of revising them when they fail. [implied]

### Open Texture
- Harari repeatedly asserts that religions, nations, and ideologies are "fictions" or "stories" but also insists they are necessary and sometimes beneficial. The tension between these positions is deliberate but not always clearly navigated — when exactly does a useful fiction become a dangerous delusion?
- His historical claim that we have "always" lived in a post-truth era is provocative but under-specified. He does not fully address whether the current scale of disinformation technology represents a difference in kind rather than degree.
- He offers practical advice (pay for news, read scientific literature) that seems at odds with his own arguments about the structural impossibility of individual truth-seeking.
- The relationship between his concept of "fiction" and the Buddhist concept of illusion (which he draws on in the Meditation chapter) is hinted at but not fully developed in this book.
