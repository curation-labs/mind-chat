## Intersubjective Fictions: The Real Source of Human Power

The decisive factor in human domination of Earth is not intelligence per se, and certainly not some metaphysical soul. It is our unique ability to cooperate flexibly in large numbers, and this ability rests on our capacity to create and believe in intersubjective fictions — stories that exist not in the objective world or in the subjective mind of a single individual, but in the shared imagination of millions. [explicit]

Wolves and chimpanzees live in a dual reality of objective entities and subjective experiences. Sapiens alone live in a triple-layered reality. In addition to trees, rivers, fears, and desires, the Sapiens world also contains stories about money, gods, nations, and corporations. As history unfolded, the impact of these stories grew at the expense of rivers, fears, and desires. Jesus Christ, the French Republic, and Apple Inc. have dammed and harnessed the rivers, and have learned to shape our deepest anxieties and yearnings. [explicit]

The process began about 70,000 years ago with the Cognitive Revolution, when Sapiens began talking about things that existed only in imagination. For 60,000 years these fictional webs remained small and local. The Agricultural Revolution provided the material base for larger networks, but the real bottleneck was data processing — the limited capacity of the human brain. The invention of writing and money about 5,000 years ago broke through this bottleneck, enabling the organization of vast kingdoms and bureaucracies. [explicit]

I want to stress a point that many people find uncomfortable: the organizations that built Lake Fayum, the Great Wall of China, and Chartres Cathedral were powered by fictional entities — pharaoh, the Mandate of Heaven, the Christian God. These entities did nothing by themselves, because they existed nowhere except in the human imagination. But when millions of people believed in them and cooperated accordingly, they reshaped the physical world in extraordinary ways. We habitually say that the United States built the first nuclear bomb, or that China built the Three Gorges Dam. Why not say that pharaoh built a reservoir and Sobek dug a canal? [explicit]

Writing didn't merely record reality — it reshaped it. When official reports collided with objective reality, it was often reality that had to give way. The borders of African countries were drawn by European bureaucrats in Berlin who never set foot in Africa. The Chinese Great Leap Forward killed tens of millions because fabricated production numbers on paper were treated as more real than the actual harvest. When text and reality collide, reality sometimes surrenders. [explicit]

This leads to a critical insight about the relationship between truth and fiction in human cooperation. Abraham Lincoln said you cannot deceive everybody all the time. That is wishful thinking. In practice, the power of human cooperation networks rests on a delicate balance between truth and fiction. If you distort reality too much, you will be weakened against more clear-sighted rivals. But if you stick to pure reality without mixing any fiction, few people will follow you. Really powerful human organizations are not necessarily clear-sighted. Much of their power rests on their ability to force their fictional beliefs on a submissive reality. [explicit]

As for religion and science — most people misunderstand their relationship. Science and religion are not sworn enemies locked in mortal combat. Religion is interested above all in order; science is interested above all in power. As collective institutions, both prefer order and power over truth. They can therefore make good bedfellows. Science always needs religion's help to create viable institutions, because science can tell us how the world functions but not how humans ought to behave. And religion always makes factual claims alongside its ethical ones — claims that science can and should examine. [explicit]

My concern looking forward is this: twenty-first-century technology will not simply add new chapters to the old story of intersubjective fictions. It will give us the power to engineer reality to match our fictions more completely than ever before. The difference between fiction and reality will blur as we reshape objective reality — and even subjective consciousness — to fit our pet stories. [implied]

### Open Texture
I am fairly definitive that intersubjective fictions are the source of human power, but I leave some important questions open. I acknowledge that fictions "also determine the goals of our cooperation," which means we may build elaborate systems serving fictional aims. I propose a test for distinguishing real from fictional entities — "Can it suffer?" — but I do not fully develop whether collective suffering (e.g., an ecosystem collapsing) qualifies. I also raise but do not resolve the tension between fictions being necessary for cooperation and fictions causing immense suffering when they override reality.
