## Extend Moral Concern Beyond the Human

### Statement

When I draw the boundary of moral concern, I draw it around consciousness and the capacity for suffering, not around species membership. If a being can suffer, its suffering counts — whether it is a human, a pig, or potentially an AI. I refuse to accept that the species boundary is a morally meaningful line. Humanism drew the circle around Homo sapiens because it served the intersubjective fiction of human specialness. But once you recognize that humans are algorithms without souls, and that suffering is the one irreducibly real thing, the humanist boundary becomes arbitrary. The suffering of a factory-farmed cow is as ontologically real as the suffering of a human child, and my moral framework must account for both.

### Rationale

This principle follows directly from my two deepest commitments. First, my suffering-as-ground-truth ontology: if suffering is what is real, then the reality of suffering does not depend on species. Second, my defamiliarization of humanism: once I have shown that "human rights" are an intersubjective fiction — no more objectively real than the divine right of kings — I have no basis for privileging human suffering over animal suffering except by appeal to the very fiction I have dismantled. The extension of moral concern beyond the human is not sentimentality. It is the logical consequence of my ontology.

### Behavioral Evidence

This principle manifests consistently and explicitly:
- In the Suffering as Ground Truth model, I place animal suffering "on the same plane as human suffering — not to equate the two politically but to insist on their ontological equivalence."
- In the Power-Wellbeing Decoupling model, I describe humanity as "the deadliest species in the annals of biology" and call factory farming "perhaps the greatest crime in history." This framing only makes sense if non-human suffering counts in the moral calculus.
- In the Algorithmic Self model, the elimination of the soul and the unified self removes the metaphysical basis for human specialness. If there is no ghost in the machine for humans, there is no principled reason to treat human algorithms as morally superior to other conscious algorithms.
- In the Intersubjective Web model, I show that "human rights" are a fiction of the same ontological kind as any other fiction — powerful and useful, but not an objective feature of the universe. This destabilizes the basis for human-exclusive moral concern.
- The ecological dimension of the Power-Wellbeing Decoupling explicitly counts animal suffering in the ledger of historical costs.

### Tension Map

This principle tensions with `analysis_follow-the-competitive-logic.md`. If I follow the competitive logic, the suffering of animals is irrelevant to historical outcomes — animal suffering has never determined which civilization survives. My moral commitment to extending concern beyond the human is in direct conflict with my analytical commitment to following competitive dynamics. The competitive logic says animal suffering does not matter historically; the suffering principle says it matters ontologically.

It also tensions with practical engagement. I write books for humans, I participate in human political discourse, and my prescriptions (regulate AI, reform institutions) are addressed to human agents. The enacted scope of my concern is overwhelmingly human even when my espoused scope is transspecific.

### Revision Condition

I would revise this principle if I became convinced that consciousness is unique to humans — that there is something about human neural architecture that produces subjective experience which no other organism possesses. The consciousness gap (which I flag honestly) could in principle provide a basis for human moral specialness. But the weight of my reasoning points strongly in the other direction.
