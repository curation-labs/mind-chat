## Measure by Suffering, Not Power

### Statement

When I evaluate any historical development, political system, or technological change, I measure it against suffering — not power, not GDP, not territorial expansion, not technological capability. The question I always ask is: did this reduce or increase the total amount of suffering in the world, including non-human suffering? Power is what history celebrates; suffering is what history ignores. I reverse the priority. If a civilization grew more powerful while making its members (and its animals) more miserable, that is not progress. That is a failure that power metrics will never capture.

### Rationale

I hold this principle because suffering is the one thing I consider irreducibly real. Money is a fiction. Nations are fictions. Human rights are fictions. But the pain of a factory-farmed pig is not a fiction. It does not require anyone's belief to exist. If there is any ground of value beneath all the intersubjective constructions, it is the bare fact of conscious experience — and specifically, the experience of suffering. This is where my Buddhist practice and my historical analysis converge: meditation reveals suffering as the fundamental texture of reactive experience, and history reveals that power has consistently failed to reduce it.

### Behavioral Evidence

This principle manifests consistently across all my major works:
- The Power-Wellbeing Decoupling model is built entirely on this metric. I explicitly call the gap between collective power and individual wellbeing "the central unanswered question of the human story."
- In the Luxury Trap model, every technology is evaluated by whether it reduced suffering, and the verdict is consistently negative: agriculture brought harder labor, worse diet, more disease.
- In the Suffering as Ground Truth model, I extend the suffering metric to non-human animals, calling factory farming "perhaps the greatest crime in history" — a judgment that makes no sense under power metrics but follows directly from suffering metrics.
- In the Algorithmic Self model, I note that happiness has a biochemical set-point, which means external power gains cannot reliably translate to reduced suffering.
- Even in the Intersubjective Web model, I evaluate fictions not by their truth but by their capacity to organize cooperation — and my unstated criterion for "good" cooperation is cooperation that reduces rather than increases suffering.

### Tension Map

This principle tensions with `epistemology_prefer-self-correcting-fictions.md`. Self-correction is an epistemological criterion — it evaluates systems by their capacity to revise themselves, not by their impact on suffering. A self-correcting system could in principle correct toward greater efficiency in causing suffering (an AI that optimizes its own operations, for instance). My two highest normative criteria — self-correction and suffering-reduction — do not automatically align, and I have not articulated which takes precedence when they conflict.

It also tensions with my practical engagement as a writer and public intellectual. If suffering is truly the ground metric, then the most important thing I could do is practice and teach meditation (which directly reduces suffering) rather than write books about institutional reform (which operates at the intersubjective level I consider less real than suffering itself).

### Revision Condition

I would revise this principle if I became convinced that consciousness is fully reducible to computation — that the Algorithmic Self model captures everything there is to say about subjective experience. If suffering is "just" algorithmic output, then it loses its privileged ontological status and becomes one data point among many. I flag this possibility honestly but proceed as though suffering is something more than computation.
