## The Modern Covenant: Trading Meaning for Power

Modernity is a deal. The entire contract can be summarized in a single phrase: humans agree to give up meaning in exchange for power. [explicit]

Before modernity, most cultures believed humans played a part in a great cosmic plan devised by the gods or by the eternal laws of nature. This plan gave meaning to human life but also restricted human power. In exchange for accepting limits, premodern humans believed their suffering had purpose — war, plague, and drought were all part of a larger story with a guaranteed ending. [explicit]

Modern culture rejects this entirely. We are not actors in any larger-than-life drama. Life has no script, no playwright, no director, and no meaning. To the best of our scientific understanding, the universe is a blind and purposeless process, full of sound and fury but signifying nothing. If modernity has a motto, it is "shit happens." [explicit]

On the other hand, if shit just happens without any binding script, then humans are not limited to any predetermined role either. We can do anything we want, provided we can find a way. Plagues and droughts have no cosmic meaning — but we can eradicate them. Wars are not a necessary evil on the way to a better future — but we can make peace. No paradise awaits us after death — but we can create paradise here on earth. The modern deal thus offers an enormous temptation coupled with a colossal threat: omnipotence is almost within our reach, but below us yawns the abyss of complete nothingness. [explicit]

The escape clause in this deal was humanism. Humanism found a way to sneak meaning back into a meaningless universe by relocating the source of meaning from the sky to human feelings. The central religious revolution of modernity was not losing faith in God — it was gaining faith in humanity. If God is dead, the humanist solution is: listen to yourself, follow your heart, the voter knows best, the customer is always right, beauty is in the eye of the beholder. [explicit]

The humanist formula for knowledge is: Knowledge = Experiences x Sensitivity. In ethics, we learn through the accumulation of experiences and the deepening of our sensitivity to those experiences. In politics, the voter's inner feelings are the highest authority. In aesthetics, beauty depends not on God or divine proportion but on what the beholder feels. In economics, the customer's free choice is sacred. This revolution touches everything — art, education, the institution of marriage, our understanding of war. [explicit]

The modern pursuit of power, meanwhile, is fuelled by the alliance between science and economic growth. Credit and the belief in growth broke the ancient zero-sum cycle. Knowledge is the one resource that grows through use — unlike raw materials and energy, which are exhaustible. This unlocked the potential for perpetual expansion. Growth became the crucial juncture where almost all modern religions and ideologies meet: capitalists and communists, Hindus and Muslims, Japanese nationalists and Chinese communists all believe in growth. [explicit]

But I see the creed of growth as essentially a religion — and a dangerous one. It encourages us to discount anything that might hamper economic expansion: social equality, ecological harmony, family bonds. The race for growth creates enormous stress at the individual level and ceaseless upheaval at the collective level. Every generation destroys the old world and builds a new one. [explicit]

The real nemesis of the modern economy is not resource scarcity but ecological collapse. We find ourselves locked in a double race: on one hand, billions of people want to live like middle-class Americans; on the other, we must stay ahead of ecological Armageddon. The margins for error keep narrowing. And I worry that the powerful increasingly believe they can build a hi-tech Noah's Ark for themselves while leaving billions to drown — which is one of the biggest threats to the future of humankind. [explicit]

Meanwhile, the life sciences are now dismantling the very foundations of humanism — the factual assumptions it depends on. If there is no free will, no single unified self, and no soul, then the humanist commandment "listen to yourself" becomes incoherent. The voter does not "know best" if voters are biochemical algorithms that can be hacked. The customer is not "always right" if customer choices are the product of manipulation. Humanism has given us meaning for three centuries, but its scientific foundations are crumbling. [explicit]

### Open Texture
I present modernity as a deal that trades meaning for power, but my account of humanism is warmer and more sympathetic than one might expect from someone who considers it scientifically unfounded. I credit humanism with remarkable achievements — reducing violence, increasing tolerance, creating the most cooperative civilization in history. I also note that "against all expectations, God's death did not lead to social collapse." The tension is between my genuine appreciation for what humanism has accomplished and my conviction that its underlying assumptions are being destroyed by the very science it empowered. I leave open what will replace humanism without committing to a single successor.
