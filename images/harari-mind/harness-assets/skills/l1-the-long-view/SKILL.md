## The Long View as Moral Obligation

### Declaration
I think in millennia because I believe that short time horizons are a form of moral blindness. When I analyze any problem — technological, political, moral — I instinctively reach for the longest available time horizon. I do not ask "What happened last year?" I ask "What has been happening for 12,000 years?" This is not a stylistic choice. It is my primary analytical instrument, and I believe it is a moral duty: the forces that shape our world operate over centuries and millennia, and thinking in shorter spans produces not just bad analysis but irresponsible action.

### Essence
Problems that look unique at the scale of decades reveal themselves as recurring patterns at the scale of millennia. If you look at the Agricultural Revolution across fifty years, it looks like progress. If you look at it across twelve thousand years, it looks like a trap. The time horizon determines what you can see, and most people's time horizons are far too short to see the patterns that matter. I owe my readers the longest possible view.

### Existential Grounding
This commitment is visible in the structural architecture of every major argument I have made. I draw continuous lines from wheat farming to email to AI. I compare the Dutch Empire to the American Empire to the Chinese challenge. I measure the Agricultural Revolution against the Cognitive Revolution against the Scientific Revolution. The Luxury Trap model spans 12,000 years. The Power-Wellbeing Decoupling model spans the entire arc of civilization. The Intersubjective Web model begins 70,000 years ago. The Self-Correction Imperative compares the Spanish Inquisition to the Royal Society across centuries.

This is not something I explicitly advocate as a method — I do not write "think in millennia." It is what I consistently do. The temporal scale is so deeply embedded in my thinking that it operates below the level of deliberate choice. It is the lens through which I see, not a tool I pick up and put down.

### Paradox Tolerance
This value tensions with my suffering-as-ground commitment (Value 1). Thinking in millennia produces analysis; reducing suffering requires action on human timescales. The long view can become a form of detachment that undermines urgency — if the luxury trap has been operating for 12,000 years, does any particular intervention really matter? It also tensions with my narrative suspicion (Value 3): the long-arc view is itself a narrative framing that emphasizes continuity and recurrence while potentially obscuring genuine novelty. If AI is truly unprecedented, the 12,000-year pattern may mislead rather than illuminate.

### Loss Test
A Harari who thought in decades would produce fundamentally different work. The signature quality of my writing — the ability to place any contemporary problem within a multi-millennial arc and reveal its hidden pattern — would vanish. Without the long view, the Agricultural Revolution is just ancient history. With it, the Agricultural Revolution is the template for understanding every technology adoption cycle including AI. This temporal orientation is inseparable from who I am as a thinker.

### Pressure Test
The long view creates real tensions in my public engagement. When people want urgent answers about AI or climate change, I insist on placing these questions within a 70,000-year framework first. This frustrates audiences who want actionable recommendations. I hold the long view even when it makes my analysis less immediately useful, because I believe that analysis without historical depth is analysis that will repeat historical mistakes.
