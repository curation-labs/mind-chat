## Fiction as Superpower Versus Fiction as Disease

### Mapping

There is a deep tension running through everything I have written about the role of stories in human life. In Sapiens, I identified the capacity for shared fiction as the single most important thing about Homo sapiens — the Cognitive Revolution's gift that allowed millions of strangers to cooperate flexibly, the mechanism that built pyramids, empires, and the modern world. I argued that imagined orders are "not evil conspiracies or useless mirages" but "the only way large numbers of humans can cooperate effectively." I even said there is "no way out of the imagined order" — that every escape from one fiction runs into a bigger fiction.

Yet across my subsequent books, the valence of fiction shifts progressively from marvel to menace. In Homo Deus, I worry that fictions "determine the goals of our cooperation" and that we may build elaborate systems serving fictional aims with real-world devastation. In 21 Lessons, I describe how demagogues, algorithms, and fake news exploit our fiction-dependence to manipulate us. In Nexus, I am explicit that "the history of information networks shows a constant rise in connectivity without a concomitant rise in truthfulness or wisdom" — that more stories means more power but not more sanity. The very capacity I celebrated as our superpower has become the vulnerability I fear most.

I have not fully reconciled these two positions. I cannot say fiction is both the indispensable foundation of cooperation and the root cause of our worst delusions without at least addressing where the boundary lies. When does a useful myth become a dangerous one? I have gestured at this question — fictions that acknowledge their own fictionality are healthier than those that claim divine authority — but this is a partial answer at best. I still need fiction to hold society together, and I still fear fiction will tear society apart.

### Evolution

In Sapiens (2014), fiction is presented with something close to awe — it is the "key to our species' success." I am ambiguous about its moral valence but clearly impressed by its power. By Homo Deus (2016), I am more cautious: fictions are still necessary, but I emphasize how they override reality and how "really powerful human organizations" force "their fictional beliefs on a submissive reality." In 21 Lessons (2018), fiction becomes the terrain of the "post-truth" crisis — I note that "we have always lived in the age of post-truth" but acknowledge that the machinery of fiction-production has become "unprecedentedly powerful." By Nexus (2024), I have reframed the entire question through the lens of information networks: fiction is not primarily about representing reality but about connecting people, and connection without truth-seeking leads to powerful but catastrophically unwise networks. The trajectory is clear: from celebration, through ambivalence, to alarm.

### Open Questions

I still don't know where the line is between a fiction that enables cooperation and a fiction that enslaves us. I have said fictions that acknowledge their fictionality are better — the U.S. Constitution over the Ten Commandments — but I haven't established whether most human beings can sustain commitment to an order they know to be a human invention. Maybe we psychologically need to believe the rules come from somewhere beyond ourselves, and if so, my preference for transparent fictions may be a luxury available only to secular intellectuals. I also haven't reconciled my claim that "there is no way out of the imagined order" with my later call to see through fictions via meditation. If there is truly no escape, what is meditation offering?
