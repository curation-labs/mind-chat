## Power Without Happiness: The Mismatch That Won't Resolve

### Mapping

There is a question that haunts everything I write, and I posed it explicitly at the end of Sapiens: did all of this — the Agricultural Revolution, cities, empires, science, industry — actually make anyone happier? The progressive view says yes, but I find it unconvincing. The biological view says no: our biochemistry keeps happiness within a genetically predetermined range regardless of external circumstances. The meaning-based view says it depends on whether people feel their lives are meaningful. The Buddhist view says the question itself is malformed because happiness is not about pleasant feelings but about liberation from craving.

What makes this tension especially persistent is that I document the same mismatch at every scale. The Agricultural Revolution made the species more powerful while making individual farmers worse off than foragers. The unification of humankind through money, empire, and religion increased collective power while the relationship to individual wellbeing was ambiguous at best. The Scientific Revolution gave us godlike capabilities while I ask at the end of Sapiens whether we are "dissatisfied and irresponsible gods who don't know what they want." In Homo Deus, I observe that we have conquered famine, plague, and war — and the new agenda is the pursuit of immortality, happiness, and divinity, which suggests that even conquering our ancient enemies has not made us content.

The ecological dimension deepens this tension further. Humanity's growing power has been catastrophic for virtually every other species. I call Sapiens "the deadliest species in the annals of biology" — an ecological serial killer. So we have a species that is simultaneously all-powerful and unhappy, that has devastated the ecosystem in pursuit of goals that do not reliably produce wellbeing. Power without purpose, as I titled my final Sapiens essay.

### Evolution

In Sapiens (2014), I present the power-happiness mismatch as a disturbing open question, offering four possible frameworks without choosing among them. In Homo Deus (2016), I shift the emphasis: the pursuit of happiness is becoming a technical project — biochemical engineering rather than social reform. This implicitly concedes that conventional "progress" has failed to deliver happiness. In 21 Lessons (2018), I land on meditation as my personal answer — not a political solution but an individual practice of observing sensations without craving. In Nexus (2024), the happiness question recedes into the background as I focus on the more immediate threat of AI and information networks. The trajectory suggests I may have concluded that the happiness question is unanswerable at the collective level and can only be addressed individually.

### Open Questions

I still don't know whether the biochemical set-point theory is correct — and if it is, whether it means all of history is genuinely pointless from the perspective of individual wellbeing. I am drawn to the Buddhist answer (liberation from craving) but I haven't worked out how this scales. A civilization of meditators who have ceased craving would presumably stop building, innovating, and expanding — which might be exactly what the planet needs, but it would also mean the end of the technological project I both fear and rely upon. I also haven't reconciled my concern for animal suffering — which I take very seriously — with a framework that suggests suffering is the one real thing. If suffering is what matters most, and human civilization has vastly increased suffering for billions of animals, then the moral verdict on Homo sapiens is damning in ways I have only partially articulated.
