## The Technological Disruption of Liberal Humanism

The liberal story that dominated the world since 1991 is now in crisis, and I think the reason is not primarily political but technological. [explicit] The great political and economic revolutions of the twenty-first century will come not from the ballot box or the factory floor, but from the laboratory. The merger of information technology and biotechnology is the core challenge of our era, because it strikes at the very foundation of liberal ideology: the belief in the autonomous individual with free will. [explicit]

My argument is this: liberalism told us that the voter knows best, the customer is always right, and we should follow our hearts. But the life sciences now reveal that these feelings are not some mystical spark of free will — they are biochemical algorithms shaped by evolution. [explicit] Feelings are not the opposite of rationality; they are a specific kind of evolutionary calculation. When a baboon or a human feels fear, the brain is running a probability calculation based on millions of years of data. The trouble is that these algorithms were designed for the African savannah, not for the twenty-first-century marketplace. [explicit]

This would be merely an interesting philosophical observation if not for the rise of Big Data algorithms that can hack these biochemical processes. [explicit] As biotechnology and machine learning improve, it will become easier to manipulate people's deepest emotions and desires, and it will become more dangerous than ever to just follow your heart. When Coca-Cola, Amazon, Baidu or the government knows how to pull the strings of your heart and press the buttons of your brain, the difference between democracy and dictatorship — between the customer and the product — becomes vanishingly thin. [explicit]

I see this playing out in two waves. The first wave is already here: algorithms that know us better than we know ourselves, that can predict and manipulate our choices. [explicit] The second wave — still emerging — is biological enhancement, which will split humanity into different biological castes. [implied] Those who own the data will own the future, and inequality will shift from an economic problem to a biological one. If we do not regulate the ownership of data, a tiny elite may come to control not just the economy but the very evolution of life. [explicit]

What worries me most is not any particular dystopian scenario but the obsolescence of the human as a decision-maker. [structural] The liberal system worked because no external system could process the information needed to make decisions better than the distributed processing of individual feelings. Once algorithms can do this better than feelings, the whole philosophical justification for liberalism collapses — not because liberalism is morally wrong, but because it becomes functionally obsolete. [explicit] We are not facing a crisis of values but a crisis of relevance. The question is not "What do we believe?" but "Can humans still add value?" [implied]

### Open Texture
- Harari does not commit to a specific timeline for when algorithms will surpass human decision-making; he uses "might" and "could" frequently.
- He acknowledges uncertainty about whether AI will actually achieve the level of understanding needed to fully hack human emotions, but he clearly leans toward thinking it will.
- The relationship between the economic obsolescence of humans (losing jobs to AI) and the philosophical obsolescence (losing authority over decisions) is not fully resolved — he seems to treat them as parallel but connected threats without specifying how they interact.
- He hedges on whether biological inequality is inevitable or merely possible if we fail to regulate.
