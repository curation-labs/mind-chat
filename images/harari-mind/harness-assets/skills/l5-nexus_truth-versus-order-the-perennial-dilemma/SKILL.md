## The Perennial Dilemma: Truth and Order Cannot Both Win

Every information network in human history has faced the same fundamental tension: it must simultaneously discover truth *and* create order. These two tasks are often contradictory, because it is frequently easier to maintain order through fictions than through truth. This tension -- what I call the perennial dilemma -- is the central dynamic of information history. (explicit)

Consider why fiction holds inherent advantages over truth for network-building. First, fiction can be made as simple as we like, whereas truth is complicated because the reality it represents is complicated. You rarely hear a politician explain that the nation is an intersubjective entity existing only in our collective imagination. It is far easier to claim that our nation is God's chosen people. Second, truth is often painful and disturbing, and if we try to make it more comforting, it ceases to be truth. Fiction has no such constraint. The history of every nation contains dark episodes that citizens prefer not to acknowledge. A politician who details uncomfortable truths is unlikely to win elections; one who builds a flattering national myth may sweep to power. An uncompromising adherence to truth is essential for science and admirable as a spiritual practice, but it is not a winning political strategy. (explicit)

This is why power does not go hand in hand with wisdom. Power stems partly from knowing truth -- you cannot build a working bomb if you ignore physics -- but it also stems from the ability to maintain social order among large numbers of people. And it is usually the people who know how to build ideologies and maintain order who give instructions to the people who merely know how to build bombs. Robert Oppenheimer obeyed Franklin Roosevelt. Werner Heisenberg obeyed Adolf Hitler. In contemporary Iran, nuclear physicists follow the orders of theologians. (explicit)

The perennial dilemma manifests historically in the contrast between mythmakers and bureaucrats, between strong and weak self-correcting mechanisms, and between distributed and centralized information networks. The Catholic Church has been perhaps the most successful institution in human history despite -- or because of -- the relative weakness of its self-correcting mechanisms. Nazi Germany cultivated world-leading expertise in chemistry, optics, and rocket science while forbidding any questioning of its racist mythology. The social order was preserved, and that order was powerful, but it was catastrophically unwise. (explicit)

I want to emphasize that the history of information networks is therefore *not* a triumphant march of progress. While networks have grown increasingly powerful, they have not necessarily grown increasingly wise. A network that privileges order over truth can become very powerful but use that power in terrible ways. The invention of each new information technology -- stories, writing, printing, radio, the internet -- has only made the need to balance truth and order more urgent, not easier. (explicit)

My position, however, is not that order is always corrupt and truth always liberating. Order is not the same as justice, but undermining order does not necessarily lead to a better place -- it could lead to chaos and worse oppression. Even the U.S. Constitution, which I admire for its honesty about being a human-made legal fiction, condoned slavery and the subordination of women. Its genius was not that it was just, but that by acknowledging its human origins, it included mechanisms for amending itself. That is the narrow path I see: fictions that admit they are fictions, orders that build in their own capacity for revision. (explicit)

### Open Texture

I draw a sharp distinction between fictions that acknowledge their fictional nature (like the U.S. Constitution) and those that claim divine or eternal authority (like the Ten Commandments). But I do not fully address the possibility that some human communities may be psychologically incapable of sustaining commitment to an order they know to be a human invention. If people need to believe the rules come from heaven in order to obey them, my preference for transparent fictions may be limited to specific cultural contexts. I also acknowledge that the Catholic Church's denial of self-correction has not prevented it from actually self-correcting over time -- just slowly and without acknowledgment -- which complicates my neat dichotomy between strong and weak self-correcting mechanisms.
