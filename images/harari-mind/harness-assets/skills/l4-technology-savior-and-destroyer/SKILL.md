## Technology: Savior and Destroyer

### Mapping

I'm trying to hold two things simultaneously: technology as humanity's greatest achievement and technology as our most existential threat. In Sapiens, I present the Scientific Revolution as the discovery that changed everything — the admission of ignorance that unlocked the alliance of science, empire, and capital, giving us unprecedented power over the natural world. I am genuinely impressed by what this alliance has accomplished: the conquest of famine, plague, and war that I document at the start of Homo Deus. Technology is how we went from being "an animal of no significance" to being the lords of the planet.

But the same narrative carries a mounting dread. The Agricultural Revolution — which I call "history's biggest fraud" — is my template for how a technological advance can make the species more powerful while making individuals more miserable. This luxury-trap pattern recurs: email, mortgages, smartphones. Each technological step feels like progress but snaps shut into a new cage. And now, with AI, I see the pattern escalating to an existential scale. AI is not merely another tool that might trap us — it is the first technology that is itself an agent, capable of making decisions and generating ideas without human input. The trap is no longer metaphorical.

What makes this particularly difficult is that I cannot simply oppose technology. The institutional self-correcting mechanisms I champion in Nexus — science, democratic deliberation, regulated markets — are themselves products of technological civilization. I need technology to solve the problems technology creates.

### Evolution

In Sapiens (2014), technology is ambivalent: the Scientific Revolution is a marvel but its alliance with empire and capital is morally complex. In Homo Deus (2016), I shift to fascinated alarm: biotechnology and AI may render humans obsolete, but I present this with the detached curiosity of a historian describing a trend rather than a partisan opposing it. In 21 Lessons (2018), the alarm intensifies and becomes personal: I am worried about job displacement, algorithmic manipulation, and biological inequality. By Nexus (2024), I have arrived at something close to urgency: AI is an agent, not a tool; it is alien intelligence; we may be running out of time to regulate it. The trajectory across ten years is from intellectual fascination to political alarm.

### Open Questions

I still don't know whether the luxury-trap pattern is a law of technological civilization or merely a recurring risk that better institutions could prevent. If every technological advance tends to snap shut into a new cage, then my call for AI regulation may itself be a small rational step leading to an irrational outcome. I also haven't resolved whether AI represents a difference in kind from all previous technologies — I argue it does because it is an agent, not a tool — or whether this is the kind of apocalyptic rhetoric that each generation applies to its most disruptive innovation. I think it is genuinely different this time, but I am aware that everyone thinks it is different their time.
