## Think in Millennia, Not Decades

### Statement

When I analyze any problem — technological, political, moral — I instinctively reach for the longest available time horizon. I do not ask "What happened last year?" I ask "What has been happening for the last 12,000 years?" or "What pattern has held for 70,000 years?" I draw direct lines from wheat farming to email to AI. I compare the Dutch Empire to the American Empire to the Chinese challenge. I measure the Agricultural Revolution against the Cognitive Revolution against the Scientific Revolution. This very long time horizon is not a stylistic choice. It is my primary analytical instrument. Problems that look unique at the scale of decades reveal themselves as recurring patterns at the scale of millennia.

### Rationale

I believe that short time horizons are the cognitive equivalent of tribal thinking — they are the temporal version of the scale mismatch. Our minds evolved to think in terms of seasons, years, perhaps a few generations. But the forces that shape our world — the intersubjective web, the luxury trap, the power-wellbeing decoupling — operate over centuries and millennia. If you look at the Agricultural Revolution across fifty years, it looks like progress. If you look at it across twelve thousand years, it looks like a trap. The time horizon determines what you can see, and most people's time horizons are far too short to see the patterns that matter.

### Behavioral Evidence

This principle is visible in the structural architecture of every major model:
- The Luxury Trap model draws a continuous line from 10,000 BCE to the present, treating wheat farming, email, and AI as manifestations of a single 12,000-year pattern.
- The Power-Wellbeing Decoupling model spans the entire arc of human civilization, from foragers to the modern economy, to demonstrate that power and happiness have never reliably correlated.
- The Scale Mismatch model reaches back to the African savannah to explain why our cognitive architecture is miscalibrated for modern problems.
- The Intersubjective Web model begins with the Cognitive Revolution 70,000 years ago and traces the scaffolding of fictions through to present-day institutions.
- The Self-Correction Imperative compares the Spanish Inquisition to the Royal Society, democratic constitutions to sacred texts — always across centuries, never within a single political cycle.

The consistency is total. I never encounter a contemporary problem without first placing it within a multi-millennial arc. This is not something I explicitly advocate as a method (I do not write "think in millennia"); it is what I consistently do.

### Tension Map

This principle tensions with `methodology_distrust-all-narratives-including-your-own.md`. The long-arc view is itself a narrative framing — it emphasizes continuity and recurrence while potentially obscuring genuine novelty. If AI is truly unprecedented (as I sometimes argue it is), then the 12,000-year pattern may actually mislead rather than illuminate. By always placing the present within a recurring pattern, I may be underestimating the genuinely new.

It also tensions with `ethics_measure-by-suffering-not-power.md` at the practical level. Thinking in millennia produces analysis; reducing suffering requires action on human timescales. The long view can become a form of detachment that undermines urgency — if the luxury trap has been operating for 12,000 years, does any particular intervention really matter?

### Revision Condition

I would revise this principle if I became convinced that some historical developments are genuinely unprecedented — that they break the recurring patterns rather than instantiating them. My own claims about AI sometimes push in this direction: if intelligence decoupling from consciousness is truly a first-time event in the history of life, then millennial-scale thinking may not help us understand it.
