## Information Is Connection, Not Representation

I want to be very clear about something that forms the theoretical foundation of everything I argue in this book: information is not what most people think it is. What I call "the naive view of information" holds that information is essentially an attempt to represent reality -- that its purpose is to give us an accurate picture of the world. According to this view, more information should lead to more truth, and more truth should lead to both power and wisdom. This sounds reassuring. It is also wrong. (explicit)

My position is that information's defining characteristic is not representation but *connection*. Information is whatever connects different points into a network. Sometimes information represents reality, and sometimes it does not, but it always connects. Consider music: most symphonies and melodies do not represent anything, which is why it makes no sense to ask whether they are true or false. Yet music does a remarkable job of synchronizing people's emotions and movements. Or consider DNA -- the molecular information that makes all organic life possible. DNA does not represent reality; there is no string of nucleobases representing "lion" in the zebra genome. Instead, DNA connects trillions of cells into a functioning network. (explicit)

This reframing has profound implications. If information were primarily about truth, then the historical success of things like the Bible, astrology, and national mythologies would be baffling. The Bible makes serious errors about human origins and the causes of disease, yet it has been one of the most influential texts in history, connecting billions of people into religious networks. Astrology is scientifically baseless, yet it has shaped the decisions of emperors and ordinary people for millennia. These are not aberrations to be explained away. They are the norm. The history of information networks shows a constant rise in connectivity without a concomitant rise in truthfulness or wisdom. (explicit)

I should stress that rejecting the naive view does not mean embracing the populist view that information is merely a weapon in power struggles. That cynical position, which I trace from Marx through Foucault to present-day populists like Trump and Bolsonaro, collapses all human interaction into zero-sum conflict. I reject this too. There is ample space between naive optimism and cynical nihilism for a more nuanced understanding. Truth is real -- I define it as an accurate representation of certain aspects of reality -- and some information networks genuinely strive for it. But truth-seeking is a special effort, not the default function of information. Without deliberate institutional work to privilege truth, the cheap and abundant fictions will always swamp the rare and expensive accurate accounts. (explicit)

The practical takeaway is this: simply increasing the amount or speed of information -- building bigger search engines, faster networks, more powerful AI -- will not automatically make us wiser. If no additional steps are taken to tilt the balance in favor of truth, more information may just mean more connectivity in the service of more delusions. The naive view has been the semiofficial ideology of the computer age, from Reagan's celebration of the microchip to Google's mission statement. My view is that it has led us badly astray. (explicit/implied)

### Open Texture

I acknowledge that truth can never fully represent reality -- even the most accurate account neglects or distorts some aspects. I invoke Borges's one-to-one map to make this point. But I do not fully resolve how much distortion is acceptable before a representation ceases to be "truthful." I also concede that the naive view is "more nuanced and thoughtful than can be explained in a few paragraphs," which suggests I may be simplifying the strongest versions of the position I am arguing against. My concept of "intersubjective reality" -- laws, gods, currencies that exist only in shared stories -- does heavy lifting throughout the book, but I leave somewhat open the question of whether intersubjective realities can be evaluated by standards beyond mere network-building success.
