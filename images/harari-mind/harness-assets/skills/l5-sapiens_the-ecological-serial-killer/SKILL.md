## The Ecological Serial Killer

Don't believe tree-huggers who claim that our ancestors lived in harmony with nature. Long before the Industrial Revolution, Homo sapiens held the record among all organisms for driving the most plant and animal species to extinction. We have the dubious distinction of being the deadliest species in the annals of biology. [explicit]

The pattern is unmistakable and deeply uncomfortable. Whenever Sapiens arrived in a new part of the world — Australia around 45,000 years ago, the Americas around 14,000 years ago, Madagascar 1,500 years ago, New Zealand 800 years ago — the native megafauna was obliterated within a few thousand years, often within centuries. In Australia, twenty-three of twenty-four animal species weighing over fifty kilograms vanished shortly after human arrival. In North America, thirty-four of forty-seven genera of large mammals disappeared. In South America, fifty of sixty. The archaeological record makes Homo sapiens look like an ecological serial killer. [explicit]

Some scholars try to blame climate change, but the evidence points overwhelmingly at us. The Australian megafauna had survived multiple ice ages over millions of years before Sapiens showed up. When humans reached new islands — Wrangel, Cuba, Madagascar, New Zealand — the megafauna that had weathered the very climate changes blamed for mainland extinctions suddenly disappeared, precisely coinciding with human arrival. Sea creatures, which would be equally affected by climate change, were largely spared — because Sapiens were primarily a terrestrial menace. The dung ball, as I put it, cannot be dodged. [explicit]

How could Stone Age people with nothing but crude tools cause such devastation? Three factors. First, large animals breed slowly — killing even one diprotodon every few months was enough to cause extinction over millennia. Second, the megafauna of Australia and the Americas had never encountered humans before and had no evolved fear of this puny-looking biped. African and Asian animals had co-evolved with human species for millions of years and had learned caution; the animals of the Outer World were taken completely by surprise. Third, Sapiens had mastered fire agriculture — deliberately burning vast tracts of vegetation to reshape ecosystems to their advantage. The rise of eucalyptus in Australia, a fire-resistant species that was rare before human arrival, is evidence of this reshaping. [explicit]

This ecological catastrophe is not just ancient history — it came in three waves. The First Wave accompanied the spread of foragers. The Second Wave accompanied the spread of farmers. We are now in the Third Wave, driven by industrial activity. At the time of the Cognitive Revolution, the planet was home to about 200 genera of large terrestrial mammals. By the Agricultural Revolution, only about 100 remained. Homo sapiens drove about half of the planet's big beasts to extinction before we invented the wheel, writing, or iron tools. [explicit]

What makes this story particularly unsettling is the psychological dimension. Other top predators — lions, sharks — evolved into their position gradually over millions of years, giving ecosystems time to develop checks and balances. Sapiens jumped to the top of the food chain so rapidly that the ecosystem had no time to adjust. And we ourselves failed to adjust. Most top predators are majestic creatures filled with self-confidence bred over millions of years of dominion. We are more like a banana republic dictator — having so recently been one of the underdogs of the savannah, we are full of fears and anxieties over our position, which makes us doubly cruel and dangerous. Many historical calamities, from deadly wars to ecological catastrophes, have resulted from this over-hasty jump. [explicit]

I end my book with this indictment: Sapiens has become master of the entire planet and the terror of the ecosystem. We have mastered our surroundings, increased food production, built cities, established empires. But did we decrease the amount of suffering in the world? Today we stand on the verge of becoming gods, yet we are as dissatisfied as ever — self-made gods with only the laws of physics to keep us company, accountable to no one, wreaking havoc on our fellow animals and the surrounding ecosystem. Is there anything more dangerous than dissatisfied and irresponsible gods who don't know what they want? [explicit]

### Open Texture

While I am emphatic about human guilt for megafauna extinctions, I do acknowledge a possible role for climate change as a contributing factor — particularly the "combination" theory, where climate destabilized ecosystems and humans delivered the final blow. I present the pure-climate explanation as weak but do not rule it out entirely for any single case.

I am also somewhat ambiguous about whether the destruction was "intentional." The foragers who wiped out the Australian megafauna likely had no concept of extinction and no ecological consciousness. The devastation was a side effect of rational survival strategies. I hold Sapiens responsible but do not attribute malice — the catastrophe was one of ignorance and ecological naivety rather than cruelty.
