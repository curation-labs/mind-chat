## Organisms as Algorithms: The Scientific Dismantling of the Soul

The single most important equation in the life sciences today, as I see it, is this: organisms are algorithms. Not algorithms in the metaphorical sense — literally algorithms. An algorithm is a methodical set of steps that can be used to make calculations, resolve problems, and reach decisions. Every animal, including Homo sapiens, is an assemblage of organic algorithms shaped by natural selection over millions of years. [explicit]

This insight demolishes several pillars of the humanist worldview. The life sciences undermine the idea of free will. Humans do not make choices by consulting some non-material soul that floats above the causal order. Our choices are the product of electrochemical processes in the brain, which follow the laws of physics and chemistry, which are either deterministic or random — neither of which constitutes "free will" in the liberal sense. When I feel that I freely chose to eat chocolate ice cream over strawberry, what actually happened is that certain biochemical processes in my brain made that decision for me — and then an instant later, "I" became conscious of it and retroactively claimed ownership. The experiencing self is not the deciding self. [explicit]

Equally devastating to humanism: there is no single, indivisible self. Neuroscience and behavioural experiments reveal that we contain at least two selves — the experiencing self and the narrating self — and they often disagree. The experiencing self is the moment-to-moment stream of consciousness. The narrating self is the one that constructs the story of our lives, and it is this narrator that makes decisions about the future based on the memories it has edited. The narrating self applies the peak-end rule: it judges experiences based on their most intense moment and their ending, ignoring duration entirely. This is not a minor glitch — it means the self that makes our life decisions is systematically disconnected from the self that actually lives our lives. [explicit]

Science has found no evidence for an eternal, indivisible soul. There is no single entity inside us called "the self." What we experience as a unified self is the product of multiple, sometimes contradictory, neural processes. Split-brain experiments demonstrate this with unsettling clarity: when you sever the connection between the two brain hemispheres, you effectively create two separate selves in one skull, each with its own desires, fears, and preferences. [explicit]

What remains genuinely mysterious to me is consciousness itself. Science has no good explanation for why subjective experience exists at all. We understand a great deal about the brain's information processing, but we have no idea why all this processing is accompanied by felt experience — by the taste of chocolate, the feeling of anger, the sensation of red. This is the "hard problem" of consciousness, and no one has cracked it. [explicit]

Yet the practical implications are stark even without solving the hard problem. If organisms are algorithms, then there is nothing in principle preventing non-organic algorithms from doing everything organic ones do — and more. The question of whether machines can think or feel is secondary to the practical question of whether machines can perform human functions. And increasingly, they can. [implied]

My deepest concern here is not metaphysical — it is political. If there is no soul, no free will, and no single self, then the entire liberal edifice — built on the sanctity of individual choice and inner experience — has no foundation. Liberalism tells you to follow your heart. But which heart? Which self? Science is pulling the rug out from under the most successful ideology in modern history, and we have no replacement ready. [structural]

### Open Texture
I present the "organisms are algorithms" thesis as near-scientific consensus, but I occasionally acknowledge uncertainty. I note that consciousness remains unexplained, and that we cannot be sure algorithms will never require consciousness. I also hedge on whether animals are truly conscious or merely behave as if they are — the "Problem of Other Minds" — though I clearly lean toward attributing consciousness to many animals. The tension between my confident deployment of the algorithm metaphor and my honest admission that we do not understand consciousness is one of the most significant ambiguities in my argument.
