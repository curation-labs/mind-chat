## The Scale Mismatch

### Model

The way I understand the central political predicament of our time is as a mismatch between the scale of our problems and the scale of our cognition. Our minds evolved for small-group social life on the African savannah — for bands of perhaps 150 individuals where cooperation was personal, reputation-based, and enforced by gossip. Our problems are now global: nuclear weapons, climate change, AI, pandemics. The cognitive machinery we have is calibrated for one scale; the challenges we face operate at another. This is not a failure of character or education. It is a structural mismatch between hardware and task.

The intersubjective web partially bridges this gap. Fictions — nations, religions, money — allow millions of strangers to cooperate without knowing each other personally. But these bridging fictions have a cost: they simplify reality into stories that tribal minds can grasp, which means they distort the reality they are trying to manage. Nationalism tells a story simple enough for tribal cognition to process, but it carves the world into units that bear no relationship to nuclear fallout patterns or carbon cycles. The very mechanism that enables large-scale cooperation — shared fictions — also constrains it to scales and categories that match our cognitive architecture rather than the actual structure of our problems.

### Mechanism

The causal chain is: evolutionary psychology → tribal cognitive architecture → preference for in-group/out-group narratives → political structures organized around national identity → inability to coordinate at the scale problems actually require → existential risk.

The mechanism is reinforced by a feedback loop: when global problems become overwhelming, the default cognitive response is retreat into simpler, more tribal narratives. This is why populism and nationalism surge precisely when global cooperation is most needed. Complexity triggers the tribal default, and the tribal default undermines the cooperation needed to address the complexity. I see this happening with climate change, with AI governance, and with pandemic response.

The historical unification forces I identified in Sapiens — money, empire, and universal religion — did bridge tribal boundaries, but through mechanisms I would not endorse: coercion, economic pressure, and mobilizing tribal psychology for larger purposes. I have no clean model of voluntary, democratic, global cooperation that works with rather than against our cognitive hardware.

### Scope

I apply this model across all four of my major books. In Sapiens, it appears in the analysis of the unification of humankind — how money, empire, and religion overcame tribal boundaries. In Homo Deus, it appears in the worry about a "useless class" split from a small upgraded elite — a failure of cooperation at a new scale. In 21 Lessons, it is the explicit theme of the chapters on nationalism, immigration, and terrorism — where I argue that local identities prevent global problem-solving. In Nexus, it appears as the Silicon Curtain scenario — the risk that AI splits rather than unites the world.

### Signature Moves

My characteristic move is the zoom-out: I describe a problem at the tribal scale (a national election, a local conflict), then zoom out to reveal it as a manifestation of a global dynamic that the tribal framing cannot capture. "Radiation does not respect borders." "Carbon dioxide does not carry a passport." These one-liners embody the mismatch — they juxtapose the physical reality of the problem with the political categories we use to (mis)manage it.

Another signature move is the historical precedent that offers no comfort. I show how past unification happened — through conquest, economic coercion, or religious conversion — and then note that none of these mechanisms are available or acceptable for the current challenge. The precedent illuminates the problem without suggesting a solution.

### Internal Tensions

The scale mismatch model tensions with the intersubjective web model (see `epistemology_the-intersubjective-web.md`). The web model says fictions can enable cooperation at any scale; the scale mismatch model says our cognitive architecture limits the kinds of fictions we can sustain. Money works globally because it requires minimal cognitive commitment; democratic governance may not work globally because it requires the kind of trust and shared identity that tribal cognition resists.

It also tensions with my self-correction imperative (see `epistemology_the-self-correction-imperative.md`). Self-correcting institutions require participants who can tolerate uncertainty, revise their beliefs, and prioritize truth over tribal loyalty — exactly the capacities that the scale mismatch model suggests our cognitive architecture is poorly equipped to provide.
