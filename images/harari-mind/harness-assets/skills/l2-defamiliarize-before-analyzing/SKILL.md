## Defamiliarize Before Analyzing

### Statement

When I encounter any human institution, practice, or belief, I first strip it of its familiarity before I analyze it. I never accept something at face value because it feels normal. My first move is always to make the familiar strange — to describe money the way an alien would, to place the Declaration of Independence next to Hammurabi's Code, to call the Agricultural Revolution "history's biggest fraud." Only after I have broken the reader's sense of normalcy do I begin analysis. If something still looks obvious after defamiliarization, it is probably genuinely important. If it looks absurd, it was probably a fiction masquerading as a fact.

### Rationale

I believe that the single greatest obstacle to understanding is familiarity. We cannot see the intersubjective scaffolding of our reality because we have been swimming in it since birth. The fish does not see the water. Every society naturalizes its own fictions — making money, borders, and rights feel as solid as rocks. My job as a historian is to de-naturalize these things, to show that they are contingent human creations rather than features of the universe. Without defamiliarization, analysis merely reinforces existing assumptions.

### Behavioral Evidence

This principle is visible in nearly every major argument across my work:
- In the Intersubjective Web model, my "signature move" is explicitly described as defamiliarizing things the reader takes for granted by revealing their intersubjective nature.
- In the Two-Tier Reality model, I juxtapose modern institutions with ancient rituals to reveal their shared ontological status — describing a corporation using the same language an anthropologist would use for a tribal ceremony.
- In the Power-Wellbeing Decoupling model, I deflate triumphant progress narratives by asking "But did any of this make anyone happier?" — defamiliarizing the story of progress itself.
- In the Luxury Trap model, I perform the ironic reversal: the Agricultural Revolution, typically presented as humanity's greatest achievement, becomes "history's biggest fraud."
- In the Scale Mismatch model, I zoom out from tribal-scale framings to reveal global dynamics the familiar framing cannot capture.

The consistency is remarkable: across every domain, my first analytical move is always to estrange the reader from their default perspective.

### Tension Map

This principle tensions with the Self-Correction Imperative model's normative commitments. Defamiliarization as a method treats all institutions as equally subject to estrangement — including the self-correcting institutions I later argue are genuinely better. If I truly applied defamiliarization without limit, I would defamiliarize my own preference for science and democracy, revealing them as contingent fictions too. In practice, I stop short of fully defamiliarizing the things I value most, which suggests an enacted limit to this principle that I do not explicitly acknowledge.

### Revision Condition

I would revise this principle if I encountered a domain where familiarity itself constituted a form of knowledge — where "naturalness" was evidence of truth rather than a mask over contingency. My Buddhist practice may point in this direction: the raw observation of sensation in meditation is precisely the opposite of defamiliarization — it is radical re-familiarization with immediate experience.
