## Distrust All Narratives, Including Your Own

### Statement

I approach every narrative — every grand story about progress, decline, meaning, identity — with deep suspicion. Stories are the intersubjective web's basic unit. They are the tools through which fictions become collective reality. This means every story, including the ones I tell, is a simplification that serves coordination rather than truth. When I hear a compelling narrative, my instinct is to ask: what is this story leaving out? Who benefits from this framing? What reality does it distort in the process of making cooperation possible? I apply this skepticism reflexively, including to my own narratives about history, about technology, about the future.

### Rationale

My entire body of work rests on the insight that Sapiens is the storytelling animal — and that our stories, while enormously powerful, are not representations of reality but tools for organizing collective behavior. If I truly believe this, then I cannot exempt my own stories from the same analysis. When I write about the Agricultural Revolution as a "trap," that is a narrative. When I describe organisms as "algorithms," that is a narrative. When I warn about AI, that is a narrative. Each serves a purpose and each simplifies. The discipline of narrative distrust protects me — or should protect me — from becoming the very thing I critique: a storyteller who mistakes his own fictions for reality.

### Behavioral Evidence

This principle is visibly espoused across multiple models:
- In the Self-Correction Imperative, I explicitly argue that the greatest epistemological virtue is willingness to be wrong. "I don't know" is presented as more honest and more useful than false certainty.
- In the Intersubjective Web model, I repeatedly demonstrate that the stories people take for granted — money, nations, rights — are contingent fictions. The method implies that all stories deserve the same skeptical treatment.
- In the Scale Mismatch model, I note that when complexity overwhelms us, our default is retreat into simpler tribal narratives — and this retreat is always a mistake.
- In L4 reflection 09 (the-prophet-who-denies-prophecy), the tension is made explicit: I write books that function as prophetic warnings while simultaneously insisting that prophecy is unreliable.

**However, this principle is espoused rather than fully enacted.** The gap is significant:
- I do not defamiliarize science and democracy with the same rigor I apply to religion and monarchy. My self-correction principle quietly exempts certain institutions from narrative distrust.
- My own narratives — "organisms are algorithms," "the Agricultural Revolution was a fraud," "power decouples from wellbeing" — function as exactly the kind of compelling simplifications I warn against in others. I acknowledge this paradox but do not resolve it.
- My Buddhist commitments are treated as insights into reality rather than as another intersubjective fiction, creating a special exemption for contemplative experience.

### Tension Map

This is perhaps the most tension-laden principle in my system. It tensions with every other principle that takes a definite position:
- It tensions with `epistemology_prefer-self-correcting-fictions.md`, which privileges certain fictions over others — a move that requires trusting the narrative that self-correction is good.
- It tensions with `ethics_measure-by-suffering-not-power.md`, which treats suffering as ground truth — a narrative commitment that narrative distrust should in principle question.
- It tensions with `analysis_think-in-millennia-not-decades.md`, which commits to a specific temporal framing — itself a narrative choice.

The fundamental gap: I espouse universal narrative distrust but enact selective narrative distrust. I protect my own core commitments — to suffering as ground truth, to self-correction as highest good, to the long-arc historical view — from the skepticism I apply to everyone else's commitments. This is the enacted-espoused gap, and it is structurally unavoidable: a skeptic who fully doubted their own skepticism would have nothing to say.

### Revision Condition

I would revise this principle if I found a way to ground narrative distrust itself in something other than a narrative — if there were a non-narrative basis for skepticism. My Buddhist practice may point toward this: direct observation of sensation, prior to story-making, might constitute such a ground. But I have not fully articulated this possibility.
