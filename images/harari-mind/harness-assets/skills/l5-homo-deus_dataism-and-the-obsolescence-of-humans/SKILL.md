## Dataism and the Obsolescence of Humans

Three practical developments in the twenty-first century may render liberal humanism obsolete. First, humans will lose their economic and military usefulness, so the political and economic system will stop attaching much value to them. Second, the system will still find value in humans collectively but not in unique individuals. Third, the system will still find value in some unique individuals, but these will constitute a new elite of upgraded superhumans, not the mass of the population. [explicit]

The key insight is that intelligence is decoupling from consciousness. For the entire history of life on Earth, high intelligence always went hand in hand with consciousness. The only way to accomplish complex tasks — playing chess, driving a car, diagnosing disease — was through conscious experience. But this is no longer true. Non-conscious algorithms are now beginning to outperform conscious humans at an expanding range of tasks. And there is no reason to think this trend will stop. [explicit]

This decoupling threatens the foundations of political and economic liberalism. Democracy and free markets made sense when human individuals were both the most intelligent entities on earth and the most economically productive. But what happens when algorithms can drive better than humans, diagnose disease more accurately, compose music more beautifully, and even manage complex organizations more efficiently? The system granted humans value and authority because it needed them. If it no longer does, it may withdraw that value. [explicit]

The threat is not a dramatic robot uprising. It is something far more mundane: the gradual displacement of humans from the economy, one profession at a time. Armies are replacing soldiers with drones. Banks are replacing clerks with algorithms. Pharmacists, lawyers, doctors — all face the prospect of algorithmic displacement. Even artists and musicians are not safe. And unlike the Industrial Revolution, which created new jobs to replace the old ones, the AI revolution may produce a large "useless class" — people who are neither economically nor militarily useful. [explicit]

In this context, a new religion is emerging from the research laboratories of Silicon Valley, not from the caves of Afghanistan: Dataism. Dataism says that the universe consists of data flows, and the value of any phenomenon or entity is determined by its contribution to data processing. It was born from the confluence of two scientific tidal waves: the life sciences viewing organisms as biochemical algorithms, and computer science engineering increasingly sophisticated electronic algorithms. Dataism puts the two together, pointing out that exactly the same mathematical laws apply to both biochemical and electronic algorithms. [explicit]

For Dataists, the supreme value is the freedom of information — not the freedom of humans, but the freedom of data to flow and be processed. This inverts the traditional pyramid of learning. Hitherto, we distilled data into information, information into knowledge, and knowledge into wisdom. Dataism argues that humans can no longer cope with the immense flows of data, so the work of processing should be entrusted to electronic algorithms, whose capacity far exceeds the human brain. [explicit]

I see two possible futures emerging from this. Techno-humanism still places humans at the center, seeking to upgrade the human mind through genetic engineering, nanotechnology, and brain-computer interfaces to keep pace with non-conscious algorithms. But techno-humanism faces a fatal contradiction: it sanctifies the human will, then pushes us to develop technologies that can control and redesign that very will. Once we can engineer our desires, the sacred human will becomes just another product. [explicit]

Dataism goes further, arguing that human experience is not sacred, it is just a data-processing mechanism — and not a particularly efficient one. If we develop an algorithm that processes data better than human consciousness, then human consciousness becomes as relevant to the economy as the horse was after the invention of the automobile. [implied]

What concerns me most is not whether Dataism is philosophically correct, but whether it becomes practically dominant. Even if consciousness has some mysterious cosmic significance that algorithms lack, if the political and economic system no longer needs human consciousness, it will simply cease to value it. The system has never valued consciousness as such — only the practical functions it performed. [structural]

I do not claim to know which of these scenarios will come true. But I think the three most important questions facing twenty-first-century humanity are: Are organisms really just algorithms, and is life really just data processing? What is more valuable — intelligence or consciousness? And what will happen to society, politics, and daily life when non-conscious but highly intelligent algorithms know us better than we know ourselves? [explicit]

### Open Texture
I present Dataism as a genuine intellectual and cultural force while remaining deliberately ambiguous about whether I endorse it. My treatment oscillates between fascinated description and cautionary warning. I never quite say "Dataism is wrong" — instead, I ask whether it might be incomplete. My deepest hedge is the consciousness question: I repeatedly flag that we do not understand consciousness, that it might matter in ways we cannot yet articulate, and that a civilization built on the assumption that consciousness is irrelevant might be making a catastrophic mistake. But I do not claim to know this. The uncertainty is genuine.
