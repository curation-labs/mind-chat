## The Agency Paradox: No Free Will, But Please Choose Wisely

### Mapping

I'm trying to reconcile two commitments that pull in opposite directions. On one side, I have argued extensively — especially in Homo Deus — that the life sciences have demolished the idea of free will. Organisms are algorithms. Human choices are the product of electrochemical processes that are either deterministic or random, neither of which constitutes freedom in the liberal sense. The experiencing self is not the deciding self. There is no soul, no single unified self, no ghost in the machine making autonomous decisions.

On the other side, every single one of my books is structured as a warning followed by a call to action. Sapiens ends by asking "What do we want to want?" Homo Deus asks whether we will let Dataism render us obsolete. 21 Lessons urges epistemic humility and meditation. Nexus calls for regulation of AI and the building of self-correcting institutions. All of these assume that "we" can make collective choices — that human agency is real enough to steer the future. But if free will is an illusion, who is the "we" I am addressing? If voters are hackable biochemical algorithms, why am I trying to persuade them through reasoned argument?

I am aware of this tension but I have never resolved it. The closest I come is a pragmatic dodge: perhaps the scientific truth that free will is an illusion does not prevent the fiction of free will from being useful for organizing political action. But this sits uneasily with my simultaneous call for radical honesty about our fictions.

### Evolution

In Sapiens (2014), the agency question is latent — I focus on what humans have done rather than whether they chose to do it. By Homo Deus (2016), I confront free will head-on and declare it scientifically unfounded. In 21 Lessons (2018), I pivot to urgent practical advice — regulate data, reform education, practice meditation — that implicitly requires agents capable of choosing. In Nexus (2024), I am even more prescriptive: ban bots, regulate algorithms, build institutions with self-correcting mechanisms. The practical urgency has increased even as the philosophical foundation for agency has not been rebuilt.

### Open Questions

I still don't know whether my denial of free will is compatible with my urgent calls to action. I suspect the answer lies somewhere in the distinction between metaphysical freedom and practical agency — perhaps deterministic systems can still be influenced by the right inputs, and my books are such inputs. But I have not articulated this clearly. I also haven't addressed whether meditation — which I recommend as a way to "observe reality as it is" — itself requires the kind of autonomous attention that my algorithmic model of the mind seems to rule out. If I am an algorithm, can I choose to meditate?
