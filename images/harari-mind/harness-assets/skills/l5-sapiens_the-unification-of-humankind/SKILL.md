## The Unification of Humankind

If you adopt the viewpoint of a cosmic spy satellite scanning millennia rather than centuries, one thing becomes crystal clear: history is moving relentlessly toward unity. The sectioning of Christianity and the collapse of the Mongol Empire are just speed bumps on history's highway. [explicit]

Around 10,000 BC, the planet contained many thousands of separate human worlds. By 2000 BC, their numbers had dwindled to hundreds. By AD 1450, close to 90 per cent of all humans lived in a single mega-world — the world of Afro-Asia. Over the next 300 years, this Afro-Asian giant swallowed all the remaining separate worlds. Today, almost all humans share the same geopolitical system, the same economic system, the same legal system, and the same scientific system. A real "clash of civilizations" is like the proverbial dialogue of the deaf — when Iran and the United States rattle swords at each other, they both speak the language of nation states, capitalist economies, international rights, and nuclear physics. [explicit]

Three great unifiers have driven this process: money, empire, and universal religion. Each represents a potentially universal order — a vision that encompasses all of humankind rather than just "us." [explicit]

Money is the most universal and most efficient system of mutual trust ever devised. It is more open-minded than language, state laws, cultural codes, religious beliefs, and social habits. It is the only trust system that can bridge almost any cultural gap, that does not discriminate on the basis of religion, gender, race, age, or sexual orientation. Christians and Muslims who could not agree on religious beliefs could always agree on a monetary belief — because religion asks us to believe in something, while money asks us to believe that other people believe in something. [explicit]

But money has a dark side. It corrodes local traditions, intimate relations, and human values, replacing them with the cold laws of supply and demand. When everything is convertible and trust depends on anonymous coins, the bonds of community, family, and sacred values are endangered. The economic history of humankind is a delicate dance: people rely on money to cooperate with strangers, but fear it will corrupt what they hold dear. [explicit]

Empire has been the world's most common form of political organization for the last 2,500 years. I know "imperialist" ranks as a swear word, but here is an uncomfortable truth: most of today's cultures are based on imperial legacies. Almost all people in the twenty-first century are the offspring of one empire or another. To colour all empires black and disavow all imperial legacies is to reject most of human culture. The language I write in, the religion of much of my audience, the legal and political systems we take for granted — all are imperial inheritances. There is no way to surgically remove the imperial legacy without killing the patient. [explicit]

The critical mechanism of empire is that over time, "they" become "us." The conquered adopt the imperial culture — its language, its laws, its gods — and eventually are accepted as full members of the imperial community. Romans absorbed Greeks; Arabs absorbed Persians and Egyptians; the Chinese Empire created the Han identity. The process is painful and often traumatic, but it is also how small, separate cultures have been welded into the larger civilizations we know today. [implied]

Universal religions provided the third great unifying force. For most of history, humans divided themselves into "us" and "them." The first millennium BC saw the emergence of orders that could encompass all of humankind: the monetary order, the imperial order, and the order of universal religions like Buddhism, Christianity, and Islam. Merchants, conquerors, and prophets were the first to transcend the binary evolutionary division and foresee the potential unity of humankind. [explicit]

I should add a critical caveat to this entire story: history's movement toward unity is not a movement toward human wellbeing. There is absolutely no proof that cultures beneficial to humans must succeed and spread while harmful ones disappear. History's choices are not made for the benefit of humans. Cultural ideas may be better understood as mental parasites — memes that replicate and spread irrespective of the costs and benefits to their human hosts. The dynamics of history are not directed toward enhancing human well-being. Like biological evolution, history disregards the happiness of individual organisms. [explicit]

### Open Texture

I am clear that unification is the direction of history, but I am deliberately agnostic about whether it is good. I present the unifying forces — money, empire, religion — as morally ambivalent. Money enables cooperation but corrodes community. Empire commits vicious slaughter but leaves behind rich cultural legacies. Religion unites millions but also divides them into believers and infidels. I refuse to resolve this ambivalence.

I am also ambiguous about determinism. I explicitly reject the idea that specific historical outcomes — why Christianity rather than Manichaeism, why English rather than Danish — were inevitable. History is a "level two" chaotic system that reacts to predictions about it. Yet I do seem to treat the macro trend toward unification itself as something close to inevitable — "an inexorable trend toward unity." There is a tension between my insistence that specific outcomes are contingent and my claim that the overall direction is determined.
