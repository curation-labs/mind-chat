## Power Without Purpose: Happiness and the End of Homo Sapiens

Here is the question that most historians never ask, and that I believe is the most important one: are we happier? The last 500 years have given humankind superhuman powers and practically limitless energy. We have gone from canoes to space shuttles. But did any of it actually make us more content? If the answer is no, then what was the point of the Agricultural Revolution, of cities, of writing, of empires, of science and industry? [explicit]

I see several ways to approach this question, and none of them is comforting. The progressive view — that human capabilities have increased, therefore happiness must have increased — is unconvincing. The Agricultural Revolution made individuals worse off even as it made the species more "successful." The spread of European empires increased collective human power while causing immense misery to millions. The correlation between power and wellbeing is far weaker than we like to imagine. [explicit]

The biological view is even more disturbing. Biologists argue that our subjective wellbeing is governed by a biochemical system shaped by evolution — a kind of internal air-conditioning that keeps happiness levels within a genetically predetermined range regardless of external circumstances. A medieval peasant's brain released serotonin when he finished building his mud hut; a modern banker's brain releases a similar amount when she makes the last payment on her penthouse. The serotonin level is the same; the penthouse is irrelevant. If this is correct, then the entire history of human achievement has had essentially zero impact on human happiness. The French Revolution, the Industrial Revolution, the moon landing — none of it changed our biochemistry. There is only one historical development with real significance from this perspective: the development of psychiatric drugs that can directly manipulate our brain chemistry. [explicit]

But I am not satisfied with the biochemical account alone. There is another dimension: meaning. Happiness may consist not in the surplus of pleasant over unpleasant moments, but in seeing one's life as meaningful and worthwhile. As Nietzsche put it, if you have a why to live, you can bear almost any how. Medieval people who believed in the promise of eternal paradise may have rated their lives as deeply meaningful, even amid terrible suffering. By this measure, the modern secular world — which offers material comfort but, from a purely scientific standpoint, holds that human life has absolutely no meaning — may actually leave us worse off than our believing ancestors. [explicit]

Then there is the Buddhist perspective, which I find most profound of all. Buddhism agrees with biology that happiness is independent of external conditions. But its deeper insight is that true happiness is also independent of our inner feelings. The problem is not that we fail to get what we want, but that we are trapped in an endless, pointless pursuit of ephemeral feelings. The mind is never satisfied — even when experiencing pleasure, it fears the feeling will disappear and craves for it to stay. People are liberated from suffering not when they experience pleasant feelings but when they understand the impermanent nature of all feelings and stop craving them. [explicit]

And now we approach the ultimate question. This book began by presenting history as the next stage in the continuum of physics to chemistry to biology. For 4 billion years, every organism evolved subject to natural selection. But at the dawn of the twenty-first century, Homo sapiens is beginning to break the laws of natural selection, replacing them with the laws of intelligent design — not divine design, but human design. Through biological engineering, cyborg engineering, and the engineering of inorganic life, we are on the verge of creating beings that are fundamentally different from anything that has existed before. [explicit]

The Frankenstein myth tells us we should be afraid of these creations. But the deeper truth the myth reveals is that the last days of Homo sapiens may be fast approaching. Our successors may possess not only different physiques but entirely different cognitive and emotional worlds. They may be to us what we are to Neanderthals — or more. We are fast approaching what physicists call a singularity: a point beyond which all our familiar concepts of self, gender, love, and hate become meaningless. [explicit]

The question I leave my readers with is not "What do we want to become?" but "What do we want to want?" We are more powerful than ever before, but have very little idea what to do with all that power. We have advanced from canoes to space shuttles, but nobody knows where we're going. We are self-made gods with only the laws of physics to keep us company, accountable to no one. Is there anything more dangerous than dissatisfied and irresponsible gods who don't know what they want? [explicit]

### Open Texture

My treatment of happiness is deliberately pluralistic — I present the progressive, biochemical, meaning-based, and Buddhist perspectives without definitively choosing among them. I seem most personally drawn to the Buddhist view (I have acknowledged a personal meditation practice elsewhere), but within this book I present it as one perspective among several rather than as the correct answer.

On the question of where Sapiens is headed, I am deliberately provocative but also genuinely uncertain. I present genetic engineering, cyborg technology, and artificial intelligence as plausible trajectories, but I am careful to note that predictions about the future are usually wrong — nuclear enthusiasts in the 1940s predicted nuclear-powered everything by 2000, but nobody predicted the Internet. My forecasts are intended to stimulate thought, not as confident predictions.

There is also a tension in my final argument: I warn that our growing power is dangerous because we lack purpose, yet I have also argued throughout the book that all "purposes" are fictional constructs. If meaning is always a delusion, then the question "What do we want to want?" may be unanswerable on my own terms.
