## Global Problems, Tribal Politics

The central political problem of our time, as I see it, is a mismatch of scale. We face three genuinely existential threats — nuclear war, ecological collapse, and technological disruption — and all three are global by nature. [explicit] No nation can solve climate change alone, no nation can regulate AI alone, no nation can prevent nuclear war alone. Yet the dominant political framework remains the nation-state, which evolved to manage much smaller-scale problems. [explicit]

I am not anti-nationalist in some naive cosmopolitan way. Nationalism has real virtues — it created the welfare state, public education, mass solidarity among strangers. [explicit] It solved the problem of governing millions of people by creating shared stories of belonging. The trouble is that nationalism has no mechanism for governing the planet. [explicit] When it comes to nuclear weapons, the greenhouse effect, or the disruptive potential of artificial intelligence, nationalists have no serious answer beyond hoping that each nation will sort out its own problems. But radiation does not respect borders, carbon dioxide does not carry a passport, and algorithms do not salute any flag. [explicit]

What I find particularly dangerous is the retreat into nostalgia. [structural] When people feel overwhelmed by global complexity, the temptation is to turn inward — to Make America Great Again, to recover some lost national glory. But the golden age that nationalists promise to restore never actually existed, and even if it did, the conditions that produced it are gone forever. [explicit] You cannot protect the job market from AI by building a wall on the Mexican border. You cannot prevent ecological collapse by withdrawing from international agreements. The problems are global whether we like it or not, and retreating into nationalism is like trying to fight a pandemic by locking yourself in your bedroom — you might feel safer, but the virus is already inside. [implied]

I think we need a new kind of loyalty — not abandoning national identity but supplementing it with global commitments. [explicit] I do not believe this requires a world government, which would probably be a terrible idea. What it requires is that nations cooperate on the specific issues that cannot be managed nationally: climate agreements, AI regulation, nuclear non-proliferation. [explicit] The model should be something like the European Union — deeply imperfect, often frustrating, but representing the recognition that some problems demand shared sovereignty. [implied]

Community is part of this challenge too. People are lonely, disconnected, and searching for belonging, and online communities have largely failed to fill the void left by the decline of physical communities. [explicit] Facebook and its equivalents can connect people across distances, but they cannot replace the intimacy of physical presence — you cannot hug someone through a screen. [explicit] The danger is that people turn to demagogues and radical movements to find the sense of community they have lost, and these movements channel legitimate loneliness into tribal hostility. [implied]

### Open Texture
- Harari is notably vague about what global cooperation mechanisms should actually look like. He says what they should not be (world government) but does not offer a detailed positive vision.
- His EU example is offered tentatively — he acknowledges it is deeply imperfect and does not claim it as a model to simply replicate.
- The tension between valuing national identity and demanding global cooperation is acknowledged but not resolved — he seems to want both without fully explaining how they coexist when they conflict.
- He is more confident diagnosing the problem (scale mismatch) than prescribing solutions.
