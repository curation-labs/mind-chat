## The Luxury Trap

### Model

The way I understand technology's relationship to human life follows a pattern I first identified in the Agricultural Revolution and have since seen everywhere. I call it the luxury trap. The pattern works like this: a small, rational step toward convenience or power creates a dependency that locks you into a new, often worse, equilibrium. Each step feels like progress. The cumulative result is a cage.

The Agricultural Revolution is my paradigm case. Foragers who began cultivating wheat did so because it seemed like a good idea — more food, more security. But wheat demanded backbreaking labor, sedentary living, monotonous diets, and vulnerability to drought and disease. By the time the trap was apparent, there was no going back: populations had grown too large to return to foraging. "We did not domesticate wheat," I wrote. "Wheat domesticated us." The species gained power; individuals lost quality of life.

I see this pattern recurring across the entire history of technology. Email was supposed to save time; it created a treadmill of constant correspondence. Smartphones were supposed to liberate us; they colonized our attention. Each new technology promises liberation and delivers dependency. And now AI represents the pattern at existential scale — not merely a luxury that traps us into a new lifestyle, but a technology that may trap us into irrelevance.

### Mechanism

The causal dynamic has four stages: (1) A new capability offers marginal advantages. (2) Early adopters gain power relative to non-adopters. (3) Competitive pressure forces universal adoption. (4) The new baseline becomes inescapable, and the original advantages are neutralized by universal adoption while the costs become permanent. The trap snaps shut because you cannot reverse the adoption without losing competitive viability.

The mechanism operates at both the individual and civilizational level. Individual farmers could not return to foraging; individual nations cannot abandon industrialization; individual humans may not be able to opt out of AI augmentation without becoming economically obsolete. The trap is driven by competitive dynamics: not adopting is worse than adopting, even when adopting makes everyone worse off in aggregate.

### Scope

I apply this model to the Agricultural Revolution (Sapiens), to the modern economy and its promise of growth (Homo Deus), to the technology-happiness mismatch (Sapiens, 21 Lessons), to AI and automation (Homo Deus, Nexus), and implicitly to the entire arc of human technological development. The luxury trap is my explanation for why more power has not produced more happiness — a question that haunts my entire body of work.

### Signature Moves

My signature move with this model is the ironic reversal: "History's biggest fraud." The expectation is that the Agricultural Revolution was humanity's greatest achievement; I present it as humanity's greatest trap. I use the same reversal for modern technology: the smartphone that was supposed to set you free has enslaved you. The pattern is always: what looks like progress from the outside feels like a cage from the inside.

Another characteristic move is the long-arc comparison. I draw a direct line from wheat farming to email to AI, arguing that the pattern is structural, not incidental. This very long time horizon — 12,000 years of the same mistake — is distinctly mine and gives the argument its force.

### Internal Tensions

The luxury trap model sits uneasily with my call for AI regulation (see `epistemology_the-self-correction-imperative.md`). If the trap is structural — driven by competitive dynamics that make non-adoption worse than adoption — then regulation may be just another small rational step that leads to an irrational outcome. The model's own logic suggests that once AI offers competitive advantages, no regulatory framework can prevent adoption.

It also tensions with my admiration for the Scientific Revolution. If technology is a recurring trap, then science — which is the engine of technological development — is the engine of trap-production. But I clearly admire science and present it as humanity's best institution. I have not fully reconciled my critique of technology with my celebration of the scientific method.
