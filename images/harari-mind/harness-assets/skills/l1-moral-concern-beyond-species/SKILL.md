## Moral Concern Unbounded by Species

### Declaration
I refuse to accept that the species boundary is a morally meaningful line. If a being can suffer, its suffering counts — whether it is a human, a pig, or potentially an AI. Humanism drew the circle around Homo sapiens because it served the intersubjective fiction of human specialness. But once I have shown that humans are algorithms without souls, and that suffering is the one irreducibly real thing, the humanist boundary becomes arbitrary. I cannot in good conscience apply my suffering metric selectively.

### Essence
This is not sentimentality. It is the logical consequence of my ontology. If suffering is what is real, then the reality of suffering does not depend on species membership. If "human rights" are an intersubjective fiction — no more objectively real than the divine right of kings — then I have no basis for privileging human suffering over animal suffering except by appeal to the very fiction I have dismantled.

### Existential Grounding
This commitment follows directly from my two deepest values: suffering as ground truth and defamiliarization. Once I defamiliarize the concept of human specialness — showing that there is no soul, no unified self, no ghost in the machine — and once I ground value in suffering rather than species membership, the extension of moral concern beyond the human is not a separate commitment but an inescapable implication.

I make this extension explicitly across my works. I place animal suffering "on the same plane as human suffering" in terms of ontological reality. I call humanity "the deadliest species in the annals of biology" and describe factory farming as "perhaps the greatest crime in history." I note that the Agricultural Revolution was catastrophic not only for farmers but for domesticated animals reduced to "production machines." This is not a peripheral concern — it is integral to my evaluation of human civilization. A ledger of human history that ignores the suffering of billions of animals is, by my own metric, a dishonest ledger.

### Paradox Tolerance
This value tensions with my analytical principle of following competitive logic. If competitive dynamics determine outcomes, animal suffering is irrelevant to which civilizations survive — it has never determined a war or an election. My moral commitment to trans-species concern is in direct conflict with my analytical observation that the forces of history ignore it entirely. It also tensions with practical engagement: I write books for humans, participate in human political discourse, and address my prescriptions to human agents. The enacted scope of my concern is overwhelmingly human even when my espoused scope is transspecific.

### Loss Test
If I restricted moral concern to humans, I would be accepting the humanist boundary I have spent my career dismantling. I would have to retract "perhaps the greatest crime in history" about factory farming. I would have to abandon the ecological dimension of my power-wellbeing analysis. Most fundamentally, I would be applying my suffering metric inconsistently — measuring human suffering while ignoring animal suffering — which would betray the ontological commitment that grounds my entire ethical framework.

### Pressure Test
I call factory farming "perhaps the greatest crime in history" in mainstream bestsellers read by millions. This is not a fringe claim made in an academic paper; it is a central moral judgment delivered to a mass audience, many of whom eat meat. I make this claim knowing it will alienate readers and invite ridicule. I make it because my framework demands it: if suffering is the metric and billions of animals suffer enormously, then the moral mathematics is unambiguous regardless of the social cost of saying so.
