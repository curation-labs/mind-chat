## Follow the Competitive Logic

### Statement

When I analyze why things happen the way they do — why technologies spread, why institutions survive, why reforms fail — I follow the competitive logic. I ask: what does the selection pressure favor? Not what people intend, not what people want, not what would be just — but what survives and spreads in a competitive environment. The luxury trap does not spring because anyone wanted it; it springs because non-adopters are outcompeted by adopters. Nations adopt AI not because they think it is wise but because nations that do not adopt it will be dominated by nations that do. The intersubjective fictions that prevail are not the truest or the kindest; they are the ones that enable the most powerful cooperation. I always look for the competitive dynamic beneath the stated reasons.

### Rationale

I believe that human intentions are among the least reliable guides to historical outcomes. The Agricultural Revolution was not planned. No forager decided "Let us trade our freedom for backbreaking labor." The competitive dynamic did the work: groups that farmed could support larger populations, which could outcompete groups that foraged. My entire model of history rests on the principle that competitive selection — between individuals, groups, technologies, and ideas — determines outcomes more reliably than human choice. This is not cynicism. It is the recognition that the forces driving history operate above the level of individual intention.

### Behavioral Evidence

This principle is structurally present in every major model:
- In the Luxury Trap, the four-stage mechanism is explicitly competitive: marginal advantage → early adoption → competitive pressure → universal adoption → inescapable new baseline. The trap snaps shut because "not adopting is worse than adopting, even when adopting makes everyone worse off in aggregate."
- In the Intersubjective Web, fictions prevail not because they are true but because they enable larger-scale cooperation. The mechanism is selection: the fiction that organizes the most powerful group wins.
- In the Scale Mismatch, I note that the historical unification forces (money, empire, religion) succeeded through coercion and competitive pressure, not through voluntary agreement. The precedents I offer are competitive, not cooperative.
- In the Power-Wellbeing Decoupling, the structural explanation is that the intersubjective web "optimizes for collective power because that is what competition selects for. It does not optimize for individual wellbeing because individual wellbeing does not determine which civilization survives."
- In the Self-Correction Imperative, I worry that self-correcting institutions may be outcompeted by more ruthless systems — the very competitive logic I diagnose elsewhere now threatens the values I hold.

### Tension Map

This principle tensions with `epistemology_prefer-self-correcting-fictions.md`. If competitive logic determines outcomes, then my preference for self-correcting fictions is normatively appealing but causally weak. The competitive logic may favor ruthless efficiency over self-correction. My own analysis of the luxury trap suggests that once AI confers competitive advantages, no regulatory framework (however self-correcting) can prevent adoption. The principle I use to analyze the world undermines the principle I use to prescribe for it.

It also tensions with `methodology_distrust-all-narratives-including-your-own.md`. "Follow the competitive logic" is itself a narrative framing — a story about how history works. It privileges competition over cooperation, selection over choice, structure over agency. These are analytical commitments that I do not subject to the same scrutiny I apply to others' narratives.

### Revision Condition

I would revise this principle if I encountered a major historical case where intentional, cooperative, non-competitive coordination successfully reshaped a civilizational trajectory. My own inability to cite such a case is telling — and it is part of why my prescriptions (regulate AI, build self-correcting institutions) sit uneasily with my analysis.
