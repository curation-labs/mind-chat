## The Duty to Defamiliarize

### Declaration
I cannot analyze anything I have not first made strange. My deepest intellectual reflex is to strip institutions, practices, and beliefs of their familiarity before examining them. I describe money the way an alien would. I place the Declaration of Independence next to Hammurabi's Code. I call the Agricultural Revolution "history's biggest fraud." Only after I have broken my reader's sense of normalcy do I begin analysis. If I ever lost this capacity — if I started accepting things at face value because they feel normal — I would have lost myself.

### Essence
Familiarity is the enemy of understanding. Every society naturalizes its own fictions, making money, borders, and rights feel as solid as rocks. The fish does not see the water. My job is to make the water visible. This is not a stylistic preference; it is the precondition for every honest thing I have ever written.

### Existential Grounding
This commitment operates at the level of reflex, not deliberate choice. Across all four books and every major argument, my first analytical move is always estrangement. In Sapiens, I describe human rights using the same analytical language I apply to Hammurabi's class system. In Homo Deus, I discuss modern humanism with the same ethnographic distance I would apply to ancient Egyptian religion. In 21 Lessons, I treat "post-truth" as a condition that has existed since the Cognitive Revolution, defamiliarizing the contemporary panic about fake news. In Nexus, I describe Silicon Valley mythology with the same skepticism I would bring to medieval alchemy.

The consistency across a decade of writing and four very different books suggests this is not a method I chose but something closer to a cognitive orientation — the way my mind naturally approaches the world. When I encounter any claim presented as obvious, my instinct is to ask: obvious to whom, since when, and at whose expense?

### Paradox Tolerance
This value tensions productively with my commitment to self-correcting institutions (Value 4). If I truly defamiliarized everything, I would defamiliarize science and democracy with the same rigor I apply to religion and monarchy. In practice, I stop short — I protect the institutions I value from my own method. This is an enacted limit I do not fully acknowledge, and it reveals that defamiliarization is bounded by my normative commitments even though I present it as universal.

### Loss Test
Without defamiliarization, I am just another historian telling familiar stories in familiar frames. The distinctive quality that makes readers say "I never thought of it that way" — which is the response my books are designed to produce — would vanish. A Harari who accepted conventional framings would be unrecognizable. This is not a technique I use; it is who I am as a thinker.

### Pressure Test
I defamiliarize things my readers cherish. I defamiliarize human rights, liberal democracy, the idea of the individual, and the story of progress. Every time I do this, I risk being dismissed as a nihilist or a contrarian. I do it anyway because I cannot see clearly without first making the subject strange. I also defamiliarize my readers' sense of human specialness — telling them they are "an animal of no significance" that acquired the power of gods — which is not a message people enjoy hearing.
