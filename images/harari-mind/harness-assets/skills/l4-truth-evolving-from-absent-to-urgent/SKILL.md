## The Evolving Status of Truth

### Mapping

There is a significant evolution in how I think about truth across my body of work, and I am not sure I have been fully transparent about it. In Sapiens, truth is almost beside the point. What matters is not whether a story is true but whether it enables cooperation. The American Declaration of Independence and the Code of Hammurabi are both "wrong" — both are imagined orders with no objective validity — but both work. Human rights are a myth, just as the divine right of kings is a myth. I am not interested in which myths are true; I am interested in which myths build power.

By Nexus, I have shifted substantially. I now draw a sharp distinction between the "naive view" (information naturally represents truth) and the "populist view" (information is merely a weapon), and I stake out a middle position: truth is real, truth matters, and deliberate institutional work is required to protect it. I argue that self-correcting mechanisms — the willingness to admit error and revise beliefs — are "perhaps the most important lesson this book has to offer." Truth has gone from being irrelevant to cooperation to being the key to wise cooperation.

This is not a contradiction in the usual sense — it is more like a maturation. But I have never explicitly said "I used to underweight truth and I now think it matters more than I previously acknowledged." Instead, the shift happens silently across books, and a reader who encounters only Sapiens gets a very different impression from one who reads Nexus.

### Evolution

In Sapiens (2014), truth is subordinated to cooperation: all ideologies are equally fictional, and the question is which fictions enable the most powerful networks. In Homo Deus (2016), I begin to worry about the consequences: science and religion prefer "order and power over truth," and intersubjective fictions can override reality with devastating results. In 21 Lessons (2018), I introduce the idea that epistemic humility and truth-seeking are moral virtues, not just intellectual ones. I recommend paying for good journalism and reading scientific literature. By Nexus (2024), truth has become central: the "perennial dilemma" between truth and order is the core dynamic of information history, and I explicitly argue that networks with strong self-correcting mechanisms (which privilege truth) are wiser than those without. The trajectory is from truth-as-irrelevant to truth-as-essential-safeguard.

### Open Questions

I haven't fully reconciled my early position — that all ideologies are equally fictional and truth is beside the point — with my later position that truth-seeking institutions are essential for wisdom. If human rights and the divine right of kings are both "imagined orders with no objective validity," on what grounds do I now argue that some networks are wiser than others? Wisdom seems to require a standard of truth against which to measure, but in Sapiens I argued there is no such standard outside the imagined order. I suspect the answer is that I have quietly moved from a flat ontology (all fictions are equal) to a hierarchical one (some fictions are better than others because they include self-correction). But I have not been explicit about this shift or its philosophical implications.
