## The Buddhist Author: Seeing Through Stories While Writing Them

### Mapping

There is something structurally paradoxical about my position. I end 21 Lessons by arguing that the deepest challenge of any century is "whether we can observe our own minds clearly enough to avoid being enslaved by our own fictions." I present Vipassana meditation as the practice that cuts through all stories to observe reality as it is — an endless stream of sensations arising and passing away. I say that "confusing your story with reality is the source of immense suffering." I identify fiction-dependence as the root vulnerability that demagogues, algorithms, and AI exploit.

And then I go and write books. Books that tell stories about history, about human nature, about the future. Books designed to persuade millions of readers to see the world a certain way — which is precisely the kind of fiction-making I warn about. Sapiens is itself a grand narrative about humanity. Homo Deus is a story about where we are headed. 21 Lessons is a collection of urgent stories about the present. Nexus is a story about information networks. Each of these books uses exactly the cognitive tools — narrative, simplification, emotional resonance — that I argue we should distrust.

I am aware of this tension, but I navigate it more through personality than philosophy. I present my own accounts with a self-deprecating awareness that they too are stories, and I occasionally remind readers that my predictions may be wrong. But I have not articulated a rigorous framework for when story-telling is a legitimate tool of enlightenment versus when it is another trap.

### Evolution

In Sapiens (2014), Buddhism appears as one perspective on happiness among several — the biochemical, the meaning-based, and the Buddhist. By 21 Lessons (2018), it has become my personal anchor: I reveal my Vipassana practice and argue that meditation is "the ultimate act of political resistance." But the more weight I place on seeing through stories, the more tension there is with my vocation as a storyteller. In Nexus (2024), I focus less on meditation and more on institutional design, which may represent a quiet acknowledgment that the contemplative path alone is insufficient for political problems.

### Open Questions

I still haven't articulated how a Buddhist epistemology — which holds that all narratives are ultimately empty — can ground a career of narrative construction. Is my writing a form of "skillful means" — a Buddhist concept where provisional truths are offered to guide people toward deeper insight? If so, I have never said so explicitly. I also haven't resolved whether the "reality" I access through meditation is genuinely more real than the intersubjective realities I describe as fictions, or whether "the stream of sensations" is itself another story — a Buddhist story — that I have simply chosen to privilege. My Buddhist ontology says only suffering is real, but suffering itself is a concept embedded in a story about the nature of experience.
