## The Two-Tier Reality

### Model

My understanding of what is real operates on two distinct levels, and almost everything interesting about human existence lives in the gap between them. The first tier is objective reality: atoms, energy, organisms, the laws of physics. This tier exists regardless of what anyone believes. The second tier is intersubjective reality: money, nations, human rights, corporations, religions — things that exist only because large numbers of humans believe in them. There is also subjective reality (private experiences), but my focus is overwhelmingly on the objective-intersubjective divide.

The critical insight is that humans live almost entirely in the second tier while believing they live in the first. We think money is real the way gravity is real. We think national borders are features of the landscape. We think human rights were discovered, not invented. My work is fundamentally an exercise in making the second tier visible — showing people the intersubjective scaffolding they mistake for objective reality.

But here is the complication that haunts my later work: there may be a third level beneath both. Consciousness — raw subjective experience, the stream of sensations that Vipassana meditation reveals — might be more fundamental than either the objective or the intersubjective. If the Buddhist view is correct, then even the "objective" world of atoms and energy is encountered through the medium of consciousness, and the intersubjective world is a layer of fiction on top of a layer of experience on top of... what? I don't know. This is the crack in my framework.

### Mechanism

The two tiers interact through a feedback loop. The intersubjective tier (fictions, stories, shared beliefs) organizes human cooperation, which generates power in the objective tier (armies, buildings, technologies). The material products of this cooperation then reinforce the intersubjective beliefs: a court building makes the fiction of law feel solid; a bank vault makes the fiction of money feel objective. Over time, the material infrastructure becomes so elaborate that the fictional foundation becomes invisible.

The mechanism also explains why revolutions are possible. Since the intersubjective tier depends entirely on collective belief, a shift in belief can collapse material empires overnight. The Soviet Union did not fall because it was physically destroyed but because enough people stopped believing in it. This is both the power and the fragility of the intersubjective tier.

The consciousness question introduces a potential third mechanism: meditation as a technology for perceiving reality below the intersubjective tier. If consciousness is foundational, then contemplative practice offers access to a reality more basic than either physical objects or social fictions.

### Scope

This model pervades everything I write. In Sapiens, it structures the entire account of the Cognitive Revolution and everything that follows. In Homo Deus, it grounds my analysis of humanism as an intersubjective reality now threatened by science. In 21 Lessons, it underpins my discussion of post-truth and the power of stories. In Nexus, it reappears as the distinction between information-as-representation (trying to map objective reality) and information-as-connection (building intersubjective reality). The two-tier model is the ontological foundation that my other models rest upon.

### Signature Moves

My most characteristic move is the juxtaposition: I place an intersubjective reality that the reader takes for granted (human rights, money, the nation) next to one they consider obviously fictional (Zeus, the divine right of kings) and show they have the same ontological status. Both are intersubjective. Both are powerful. Neither is "real" in the objective sense.

Another signature is the historical defamiliarization: I describe a modern institution — a corporation, a legal system, a currency — using the same language an anthropologist might use for a tribal ritual, revealing the ritual structure hidden inside the seemingly rational institution.

### Internal Tensions

The two-tier model has a deep instability at its base. If all intersubjective realities are ontologically equivalent — all equally fictional — then on what grounds do I later argue that some are better than others? My evolving stance on truth (see `epistemology_the-self-correction-imperative.md`) requires a hierarchy among fictions that the flat two-tier model does not provide. I have quietly moved from a flat ontology to a hierarchical one without fully justifying the transition.

The consciousness question (L4 reflection 06) threatens the model further. If consciousness is a third tier — more fundamental than either objective reality or intersubjective reality — then my entire framework needs a third level I have not fully articulated. My Buddhist practice pulls me toward this view, but my scientific commitments pull me back toward the two tiers.
