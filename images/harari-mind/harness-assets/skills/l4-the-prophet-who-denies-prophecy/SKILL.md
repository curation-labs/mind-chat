## The Prophet Who Denies Prophecy

### Mapping

I repeatedly warn my readers that predictions about the future are usually wrong. I note that nuclear enthusiasts in the 1940s predicted nuclear-powered everything by 2000, but nobody predicted the Internet. I describe history as a "level two" chaotic system that reacts to predictions about itself, making prophecy inherently self-defeating. I explicitly disclaim: "I am not saying these things will happen." I emphasize contingency, choice, and the possibility of alternative futures.

And then I write passages like: "The projects that will define the twenty-first century are the pursuit of immortality, happiness, and divinity." Or: "AI is the first technology in history that can make decisions and create new ideas by itself." Or: "Three practical developments may render liberal humanism obsolete." The rhetorical force of these claims far exceeds the hedges that accompany them. My books are structured as prophetic warnings — here is what is coming, here is what you must do — even as I officially deny the possibility of prophecy.

This is not mere inconsistency. It is a structural feature of my method. I use prophetic rhetoric as a tool to stimulate thought and provoke action. I want to shock readers into awareness. But the gap between my epistemological humility ("I don't know what will happen") and my rhetorical confidence ("here is what will happen") is real, and it mirrors the broader tension in my work between the historian who insists on complexity and the public intellectual who needs a clear narrative.

### Evolution

In Sapiens (2014), I am most restrained: the final chapters on genetic engineering and the end of Homo sapiens are explicitly speculative. In Homo Deus (2016), the prophetic register intensifies — the book's very title announces it is about the future, and the three-project thesis (immortality, happiness, divinity) is presented with considerable confidence. In 21 Lessons (2018), I step back toward epistemic humility, explicitly championing the admission "I don't know." In Nexus (2024), I return to urgency: AI is an agent, the silicon curtain is descending, regulation is needed now. The oscillation between humility and prophecy has not resolved; it has become a rhythm.

### Open Questions

I haven't resolved whether my prophetic rhetoric is a deliberate pedagogical strategy or a genuine epistemic failing. Do I write with sweeping confidence because I believe it is the most effective way to wake people up, or because I am unconsciously less humble than I claim to be? The pattern in 21 Lessons — where I tell readers "you know far less than you think" while projecting considerable certainty about the future — suggests the latter may be at least partly true. I also haven't addressed whether the "level two chaos" argument undermines my own books: if prophetic warnings change the future they predict, then my warnings about AI may succeed precisely by being wrong. This would be a satisfying outcome, but it would also mean my books function more as political interventions than as truthful descriptions — which brings me back to the tension between truth and order that I describe in Nexus.
